# Cognitive Bias Types

Cognitive biases are systematic patterns of deviation from norm or rationality in judgment. They affect how we think, decide, and act. Understanding these biases helps us recognize when our thinking might be distorted.

## Overview

Cognitive biases evolved as mental shortcuts to help us process information quickly. In modern environments, these shortcuts often lead us astray. Understanding the different types helps us recognize and mitigate their effects.

**Key principle**: Knowing about a bias doesn't eliminate it. But it helps us recognize when it might be affecting our thinking.

## Core Cognitive Biases

### 1. Confirmation Bias

**Definition**: The tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information that confirms one's existing beliefs.

**How it works**: Our brains prefer consistency. We naturally seek information that supports what we already believe and dismiss contradictory evidence.

**Manifestations**:

- Only reading articles that agree with your views
- Interpreting ambiguous data as supporting your conclusion
- Remembering hits and forgetting misses
- Dismissing sources that challenge your views

**Examples**:

- An investor only reads bullish research and ignores bearish signals
- A politician focuses on news that supports their position
- A product manager interprets user feedback selectively
- A hiring manager notices resume details that confirm expectations

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Actively seek contradictory evidence
- Appoint devil's advocates
- Ask: "What would change my mind?"
- Require explicit consideration of alternatives

**Key question**: "Am I testing my idea or trying to prove it?"

---

### 2. Availability Heuristic

**Definition**: Overestimating the importance of information that comes to mind easily.

**How it works**: Things that are memorable, recent, or emotionally charged seem more common and important than they actually are.

**Manifestations**:

- Overestimating risks of rare events with media coverage
- Making decisions based on the most recent information
- Being influenced by compelling stories over data
- Underestimating common risks because they're mundane

**Examples**:

- Fear of flying after a plane crash while driving becomes more dangerous
- Overestimating startup success rates after reading about successful exits
- Making big decisions after a powerful conversation
- Adding features because one customer requested them prominently

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Use base rates and statistical data
- Look at actual frequencies, not vivid examples
- Create distance before major decisions
- Build systematic processes

**Key question**: "Am I reacting to what's memorable or what's likely?"

---

### 3. Anchoring Bias

**Definition**: Relying too heavily on the first piece of information encountered.

**How it works**: The first information we receive creates a mental anchor. Subsequent adjustments are biased toward that anchor, even when it's arbitrary.

**Manifestations**:

- Negotiations heavily influenced by the first number
- Business valuations based on initial estimates
- Pricing decisions anchored to competitor prices
- Planning estimates anchored to first impressions

**Examples**:

- Accepting a lowball salary offer because it's close to your "number"
- A startup's valuation staying anchored to first investment
- Product pricing influenced by initial competitor analysis
- Project timelines anchored to first estimate

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Start analysis without anchors
- Use multiple anchors
- Ask: "What would I think without this information?"
- Consider the problem from scratch

**Key question**: "Would I make the same decision if the anchor were different?"

---

### 4. Overconfidence Effect

**Definition**: Overestimating one's own abilities, knowledge, or predictions.

**How it works**: We tend to think we're better than average and more accurate than we are.

**Manifestations**:

- Underestimating project timelines
- Overestimating ability to execute
- Overestimating market opportunities
- Confidence not matching track record

**Examples**:

- 90% of drivers think they're above-average drivers
- Software projects estimated at 3 months typically take 6+
- Most entrepreneurs think they have above-average chances of success
- Prediction confidence often 2x higher than accuracy

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Use reference class forecasting
- Build in explicit buffers
- Track calibration over time
- Get outside estimates

**Key question**: "What's my track record on similar predictions?"

---

### 5. Sunk Cost Fallacy

**Definition**: Continuing to invest in something because of past investment rather than future value.

**How it works**: Human beings hate waste. We continue throwing resources at failing projects because stopping means admitting that previous resources were wasted.

**Manifestations**:

- Finishing a book you hate because you started it
- Continuing failed projects because of past investment
- Staying in bad relationships because of history
- Not pivoting despite clear evidence of failure

**Examples**:

- Continuing a failing product because "we've invested so much"
- A company spending more to complete a project than it's worth
- Refusing to abandon a strategy despite poor results
- Hiring more people into a failing initiative

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Focus only on marginal costs and benefits
- Frame decisions as going forward only
- Ask: "If I were starting fresh today, would I invest here?"
- Separate decision-maker from original decision

**Key question**: "Would I make this same investment if I were starting today?"

---

### 6. Hindsight Bias

**Definition**: Seeing past events as having been predictable after they've occurred.

**How it works**: Once we know the outcome, we believe we "knew it all along."

**Manifestations**:

- "I knew this would happen"
- Overestimating predictability of events
- Being overcritical of others' decisions
- Not learning from experience properly

**Examples**:

- Judging past decisions without considering what was known then
- "Everyone saw the crisis coming" when they didn't
- Being overconfident in predicting future events
- Not giving fair weight to uncertainty in past decisions

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Document predictions before outcomes are known
- Review decisions with "before" information
- Focus on decision process, not just outcomes
- Be explicit about what was actually predictable

**Key question**: "What did I actually know at the time, not what do I know now?"

---

### 7. Dunning-Kruger Effect

**Definition**: Overestimating one's competence in areas where one is actually incompetent.

**How it works**: The same knowledge needed to recognize competence is needed to know you lack it.

**Manifestations**:

- Inexperienced people often being most confident
- Experts underestimating their blind spots
- Beginners overestimating their abilities
- novices not recognizing complexity

**Examples**:

- A new manager thinking management is easy
- A beginner coder thinking they've mastered programming
- A first-time founder underestimating challenges
- Someone reading one book thinking they're an expert

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Seek feedback from experts
- Recognize limits of your knowledge
- Study what experts actually know
- Track your actual performance

**Key question**: "Am I competent enough to recognize my incompetence?"

---

### 8. Survivorship Bias

**Definition**: Focusing on successful examples while ignoring failed ones.

**How it works**: We see what's visible (success) and don't see what's hidden (failure).

**Manifestations**:

- Studying successful companies while ignoring failures
- Learning only from winners' strategies
- Assuming success patterns are causative
- Ignoring base rates

**Examples**:

- "Just do what successful entrepreneurs did" ignoring that many did the same and failed
- Learning marketing from brands that got lucky
- Taking advice from successful people who had survivorship luck
- Ignoring all the companies that failed with similar strategies

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Study failures, not just successes
- Look at base rates
- Understand what everyone tried, not just who succeeded
- Consider selection effects

**Key question**: "Am I only looking at survivors?"

---

### 9. Bandwagon Effect

**Definition**: Doing something because others are doing it.

**How it works**: When uncertain, we look to others for guidance, which can lead us to follow the crowd even when the crowd is wrong.

**Manifestations**:

- Following business trends without analysis
- Imitating competitor moves
- "Everyone is doing this"
- FOMO-driven decisions

**Examples**:

- Adding AI to products because competitors are doing it
- Chasing markets because VCs are funding them
- Implementing practices because famous companies do them
- Investing in bubbles because everyone else is

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Understand your specific context
- Make decisions based on first principles
- Ask why others are doing something, not just what
- Recognize when you're following vs. leading

**Key question**: "Does this make sense for our specific situation?"

---

### 10. Loss Aversion

**Definition**: Preferring to avoid losses over acquiring equivalent gains.

**How it works**: Losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good.

**Manifestations**:

- Holding onto losing positions to avoid realizing losses
- Overvaluing what we have
- Avoiding risks even when they're favorable
- Fear of regret stronger than hope of gain

**Examples**:

- Refusing to pivot because it feels like admitting failure
- Staying in bad situations to avoid the pain of change
- Missing good opportunities to avoid potential bad outcomes
- Neglecting upside to protect downside

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Reframe losses as opportunities
- Set decision criteria in advance
- Separate emotional response from rational analysis
- Consider expected value

**Key question**: "Am I being irrational about losses?"

---

## Decision-Making Biases

### 11. Status Quo Bias

**Definition**: Preferring the current state over change, even when change would be beneficial.

**How it works**: Change requires effort and carries risk. The default feels safer.

**Manifestations**:

- Continuing outdated processes
- Keeping underperforming employees
- Maintaining failed strategies
- Resistance to improvements

**Examples**:

- Using legacy software because "it works"
- Keeping team members who should move on
- Maintaining products with declining usage
- Avoiding necessary organizational changes

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Treat status quo as one option
- Ask: "If we weren't doing this, would we start?"
- Create explicit decision points
- Appoint someone to argue for change

**Key question**: "If we weren't doing this today, would we choose to start?"

---

### 12. Planning Fallacy

**Definition**: Underestimating time, costs, and risks while overestimating benefits.

**How it works**: When planning, we focus on best-case execution rather than typical problems.

**Manifestations**:

- Projects that take twice as long as estimated
- Budgets that are systematically exceeded
- Plans assuming everything goes right
- Launch dates that slip

**Examples**:

- Software projects estimated at 3 months taking 6-9 months
- Product launches that keep getting delayed
- Strategic plans that assume perfect execution
- Real estate projects over budget by 50%+

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Use reference class forecasting
- Add explicit buffers
- Break projects into smaller pieces
- Learn from actuals

**Key question**: "What did similar projects actually take vs. estimate?"

---

### 13. Gambler's Fallacy

**Definition**: Believing that future probabilities are affected by past events.

**How it works**: Random events seem to even out, but they don't.

**Manifestations**:

- Assuming a "due" outcome after a streak
- Thinking past events affect random chance
- Investment decisions based on "hot streaks"
- Business decisions based on luck patterns

**Examples**:

- An investor thinking a stock is "due" after a losing streak
- A gambler thinking they're "lucky" after a win
- A business thinking they've "paid their dues" with failures
- Assuming that because things went wrong, they'll go right

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Recognize that independent events don't affect each other
- Use base rates, not recent experience
- Make decisions based on probabilities, not feelings
- Understand variance

**Key question**: "Do past events actually affect future probabilities?"

---

### 14. False Consensus

**Definition**: Overestimating how much others agree with our views.

**How it works**: We assume others see things the way we do, especially in like-minded groups.

**Manifestations**:

- Assuming stakeholders are aligned without asking
- Making product decisions based on "users want this"
- Thinking decisions have consensus when they don't
- Overestimating support for your views

**Examples**:

- A founder assuming the team is aligned without discussion
- Product features built because "customers have been asking"
- Executive decisions made assuming board support
- Startups launching features that users don't actually want

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Actually ask people their opinions
- Test assumptions with data
- Create safe spaces for disagreement
- Use anonymous input

**Key question**: "Who have I actually asked, and what did they say?"

---

### 15. Outcome Bias

**Definition**: Judging a decision by its outcome rather than the quality of the decision at the time.

**How it works**: Good outcomes make us think good decisions were made, even if they were risky.

**Manifestations**:

- Praising leaders for lucky outcomes
- Rewarding results without examining process
- Learning wrong lessons from success
- Blaming leaders for bad luck

**Examples**:

- Crediting a leader for market timing luck
- Rewarding a successful launch that was high-risk
- Blaming a failed pivot when it was the right decision
- Not learning from near-misses that turned out okay

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Judge decisions by process, not just outcome
- Distinguish between luck and skill
- Reward good decisions even when outcomes are bad
- Consider what was known at decision time

**Key question**: "Would I judge this decision the same way if the outcome were different?"

## Social Biases

### 16. Authority Bias

**Definition**: Overvaluing the opinions of authority figures.

**How it works**: We give too much weight to what authorities say.

**Manifestations**:

- Following expert advice without questioning
- Not challenging leadership views
- Accepting credentials over substance
- Groupthink around leaders

**Examples**:

- Implementing practices because a consultant recommended them
- Following industry "experts" without analysis
- Not questioning executives' assumptions
- Trusting experts outside their domain

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Evaluate ideas on their merits
- Ask: "Is this expert right?"
- Consider credentials vs. substance
- Seek multiple authoritative views

**Key question**: "Is this person an expert in this specific area?"

---

### 17. Halo Effect

**Definition**: Letting one positive trait influence judgments of other traits.

**How it works**: One outstanding quality makes everything seem better.

**Manifestations**:

- Attractive people seeming more competent
- Successful companies seeming infallible
- Famous people seeming expert in everything
- One achievement implying overall capability

**Examples**:

- Hiring the "star" from a successful company
- Believing a company's new product will succeed because past products did
- Thinking a charismatic speaker is knowledgeable
- Overestimating the competence of impressive people

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Evaluate traits independently
- Look at specific evidence
- Consider the halo effect
- Test claims separately

**Key question**: "Am I letting one trait influence my overall judgment?"

---

### 18. Fundamental Attribution Error

**Definition**: Attributing others' behavior to their character rather than circumstances.

**How it works**: We blame people for problems that are really caused by situations.

**Manifestations**:

- "He's just lazy" when the system doesn't incentivize work
- "She doesn't care" when the process makes caring hard
- "They're incompetent" when they're set up to fail
- Personal blame for systemic problems

**Examples**:

- Blaming employees for failures caused by poor management
- Attributing poor performance to character rather than circumstances
- Not recognizing situational factors in others' behavior
- Making character judgments without understanding context

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Consider situational factors
- Ask: "What circumstances might cause this?"
- Recognize you don't see the full picture
- Separate person from behavior

**Key question**: "What situational factors might be affecting this?"

---

### 19. In-Group Bias

**Definition**: Favoring people who are like us or in our group.

**How it works**: We naturally favor those who seem similar to us.

**Manifestations**:

- Hiring people who are like us
- Trusting "insiders" over "outsiders"
- Giving more weight to internal opinions
- Preferring familiar approaches

**Examples**:

- "Culture fit" often meaning cultural similarity
- Prioritizing internal candidates over external
- Overvaluing opinions from the "team"
- Discounting external perspectives

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Actively seek diverse perspectives
- Question similarity preferences
- Evaluate based on merit, not similarity
- Include outsiders

**Key question**: "Am I favoring this person because they're good, or because they're like me?"

---

### 20. Groupthink

**Definition**: Prioritizing consensus over critical evaluation.

**How it works**: The desire for harmony overrides realistic appraisal.

**Manifestations**:

- Not expressing concerns to maintain harmony
- Decisions made without critical evaluation
- Pressure on dissenters to conform
- Illusion of unanimity

**Examples**:

- Board decisions that everyone agrees with but are flawed
- Management teams that don't challenge each other
- Product decisions without real debate
- Strategic pivots without proper analysis

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Appoint devil's advocates
- Create anonymous input mechanisms
- Encourage dissenting views
- Break into subgroups

**Key question**: "Is this real agreement or compliance?"

---

## Memory and Perception Biases

### 21. Recency Bias

**Definition**: Overweighting recent events over historical data.

**How it works**: What's fresh in memory seems more important.

**Manifestations**:

- Making decisions based on what just happened
- Forgetting important historical context
- Overweighting recent performance
- Chasing recent trends

**Examples**:

- Promoting the person who just succeeded
- Abandoning a strategy because recent results were poor
- Overestimating likelihood of recent events
- Forgetting lessons from history

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Look at longer time horizons
- Track patterns over time
- Document historical context
- Challenge recency weighting

**Key question**: "What's the historical pattern, not just recent events?"

---

### 22. Peak-End Rule

**Definition**: Judging experiences by the peak moment and ending rather than the whole.

**How it works**: The best/worst moment and ending dominate memory.

**Manifestations**:

- Product experiences judged by best/worst moments
- Performance reviews dominated by recent events
- Project retrospectives focused on climax
- Customer satisfaction based on endings

**Examples**:

- A great ending salvages a poor overall experience
- A brilliant finish makes people forget poor middle
- Recent failures dominate perception of overall performance
- Product launches remembered by launch day, not results

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Consider the entire experience
- Use objective metrics
- Capture feedback at multiple points
- Focus on averages, not peaks

**Key question**: "Am I judging by peaks and endings rather than overall?"

---

### 23. Clustering Illusion

**Definition**: Seeing patterns in random events that aren't actually there.

**How it works**: Random events seem non-random, leading to false conclusions.

**Manifestations**:

- Finding meaning in noise
- Creating causation from correlation
- Seeing trends in small samples
- Interpreting random success as skill

**Examples**:

- A "hot hand" in sports that is actually random
- Finding patterns in market data that are noise
- Creating strategy from a few data points
- Attributing success to specific actions when luck was involved

**Mitigation strategies**:

- Consider sample sizes
- Use statistical significance
- Distinguish correlation from causation
- Recognize variance

**Key question**: "Is this pattern real or just random noise?"

---

## Summary

**Key principles**:

1. **Biases are systematic, not random**: They follow patterns we can recognize
2. **Knowing about bias doesn't eliminate it**: Awareness helps but doesn't fix
3. **Biases often work together**: Multiple biases often affect one decision
4. **Context matters**: When biases are most damaging

**Common threads**:

- Overconfidence in our judgment
- Seeking information that confirms
- Ignoring information that contradicts
- Being influenced by what's easy to remember
- Prioritizing consistency over accuracy

**The path forward**:

1. **Recognize**: Know the bias patterns
2. **Acknowledge**: Assume you're biased
3. **Structure**: Build processes to reduce bias
4. **Seek**: Get outside input
5. **Track**: Measure your accuracy
6. **Improve**: Learn from mistakes

**The goal**: Not to eliminate bias, but to recognize when it's likely affecting your decisions and take appropriate countermeasures.
