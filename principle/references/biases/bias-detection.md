# Bias Detection

Cognitive biases systematically distort our thinking. Recognizing when bias might be affecting your decisions is the first step to mitigating its impact. This guide provides frameworks and techniques for detecting bias in yourself and others.

## Overview

**What are cognitive biases?** Mental shortcuts that lead to systematic errors in judgment. They're not random mistakes—they follow patterns that are predictable once you know what to look for.

**Why detection matters**: You can't fix what you can't see. Understanding bias patterns helps you recognize when they might be influencing your thinking.

**The goal**: Not to eliminate bias (impossible) but to recognize when bias is likely affecting your decisions and take appropriate countermeasures.

## Detection Frameworks

### The Pattern Recognition Approach

Look for these common patterns that indicate bias might be present:

**High confidence with weak evidence**

- Making definitive statements without data
- "I know this will work"
- Ignoring uncertainty
- Resistance to contradictory information

**Emotional involvement**

- Strong emotional reactions to questions
- Defensive responses
- Taking feedback personally
- Wanting a particular outcome

**Single-source information**

- Only considering one perspective
- No diverse viewpoints consulted
- Only talking to people who agree
- Data from one biased source

**Recent experience weighting**

- Overemphasizing what just happened
- Generalizing from recent events
- Ignoring historical patterns
- "This time is different"

**Anchoring on initial information**

- First piece of information dominates
- Difficulty updating from new data
- Relative comparisons that don't matter
- Status quo preference

---

### The Decision Quality Check

Before major decisions, assess the quality of your decision process:

**Inputs**

- Are sources diverse and reliable?
- Have you sought contradictory evidence?
- Is information current and relevant?
- Are you aware of what you don't know?

**Process**

- Have you used structured decision frameworks?
- Did you define criteria before evaluating options?
- Have you challenged your assumptions?
- Did you make decisions at the right pace?

**Outputs**

- Are you considering multiple options?
- Are probabilities and uncertainties acknowledged?
- Have you tested for common biases?
- Is there appropriate hedging?

---

### The Observer Detection Method

Sometimes others can see our biases more clearly than we can:

**Questions to ask trusted others**:

- "What am I missing in this situation?"
- "What would you do if you were in my position?"
- "Am I being objective about this?"
- "What biases might be affecting my thinking?"

**Red flags others might notice**:

- You dismiss certain information too quickly
- Your reasoning seems circular
- You're overly confident in uncertain situations
- You're solving for yourself, not the problem

---

### The Historical Check

Compare current thinking to past patterns:

**Questions to ask**:

- "Have I been here before? What happened?"
- "What did I learn from similar situations?"
- "Am I repeating past mistakes?"
- "What's different this time, and am I sure?"

**Warning signs**:

- "This time is different" without evidence
- Ignoring lessons from the past
- Overriding good historical data
- Not having a track record of checking

## Bias-Specific Detection

### Confirmation Bias Detection

**Signs you might be experiencing confirmation bias**:

- You seek information that supports your view
- You interpret ambiguous data as supporting you
- You dismiss contrary evidence
- You're not seeking out skeptics

**Questions to test for confirmation bias**:

1. "What evidence would change my mind?"
2. "Who disagrees with me and why?"
3. "Have I actively sought contrary views?"
4. "Am I testing my idea or trying to prove it?"

**Countermeasures**:

- Appoint a devil's advocate
- Write down what would have to be true for your view to be wrong
- Require evidence for claims
- Track your track record on predictions

---

### Overconfidence Bias Detection

**Signs of overconfidence**:

- Making precise predictions without wide error ranges
- Not updating beliefs when new data arrives
- Assuming you're less biased than others
- Disregarding base rates

**Questions to test for overconfidence**:

1. "What's the range of possible outcomes?"
2. "How confident am I, and why?"
3. "What's my track record on similar predictions?"
4. "What could I be wrong about?"

**Countermeasures**:

- Use calibration exercises
- Consider base rates
- Seek outside estimates
- Build in explicit buffers

---

### Sunk Cost Bias Detection

**Signs of sunk cost thinking**:

- Continuing because of past investment
- "We've come this far"
- Difficulty admitting past decisions were wrong
- Continuing to invest to avoid waste feelings

**Questions to test for sunk cost**:

1. "If I were starting fresh today, would I make the same choice?"
2. "Am I considering future value, not past investment?"
3. "What would I do if I'd just encountered this situation?"
4. "What's the additional return on continued investment?"

**Countermeasures**:

- Separate decision-maker from original decision-maker
- Consider write-offs explicitly
- Frame decisions as going forward only
- Require fresh perspective on ongoing investments

---

### Anchoring Bias Detection

**Signs of anchoring**:

- Being heavily influenced by initial information
- Difficulty adjusting from a first number
- Relative comparisons that don't matter
- Status quo feeling "right"

**Questions to test for anchoring**:

1. "What would I think if the initial information were different?"
2. "Am I anchored on something irrelevant?"
3. "What would an outsider think?"
4. "Have I considered the problem without this anchor?"

**Countermeasures**:

- Start analysis fresh without anchors
- Consider multiple anchors
- Use first-principles reasoning
- Ask: "What would I think if I didn't know this?"

---

### Availability Bias Detection

**Signs of availability bias**:

- Overweighting recent information
- Vivid events seeming more likely
- Making decisions based on what's easy to remember
- Ignoring statistical base rates

**Questions to test for availability**:

1. "Am I reacting to what's memorable rather than likely?"
2. "What's the statistical base rate?"
3. "Am I overweighting recent events?"
4. "What data do I have beyond what I remember?"

**Countermeasures**:

- Use data over anecdotes
- Consider base rates explicitly
- Wait before reacting to vivid events
- Track patterns over time

---

## Detection in Group Settings

### Meeting Detection

Watch for these bias patterns in group discussions:

**Groupthink indicators**:

- No disagreement expressed
- Quick consensus
- Pressure on dissenters to conform
- Decision-makers isolated from outside views

**Premature convergence**:

- Decision made before all information shared
- Alternatives not fully explored
- Pressure to move forward
- "Let's just decide"

**Authority bias**:

- Following the loudest or highest-ranked voice
- Not challenging leadership views
- Silence interpreted as agreement
- Critical questions not asked

---

### Team Decision Detection

**Signs of collective bias**:

- Everyone agrees too easily
- Similar perspectives dominate
- Alternative views not explored
- Past successes create overconfidence

**Red flags for team decisions**:

- No devil's advocate
- Limited diversity of view
- Pressure for consensus
- Fast decisions on complex topics

## Self-Detection Techniques

### Pre-Mortem for Bias

Before any major decision, conduct a bias-focused pre-mortem:

1. **Imagine the decision has failed badly**
2. **Ask**: "What biases might have contributed to this failure?"
3. **List specific biases** that could have affected the decision
4. **For each bias, ask**: "How would we detect and mitigate this?"
5. **Build in countermeasures** before proceeding

### Decision Journal

Keep a record of decisions and outcomes:

**What to record**:

- The decision and reasoning
- Key information considered
- Assumptions made
- Confidence level
- Predicted outcomes

**What to track**:

- Were predictions accurate?
- What biases might have affected decisions?
- What would you do differently?
- What's your calibration?

### Calibration Practice

Test and improve your confidence calibration:

1. Make predictions with explicit probability estimates
2. Track accuracy over time
3. Compare confidence to actual results
4. Adjust based on track record

## Detection Question Templates

Use these questions to surface potential bias:

### General Bias Check

- "What biases might be affecting my thinking here?"
- "What am I assuming that might not be true?"
- "What information am I ignoring or underweighting?"
- "Am I solving the right problem?"

### Information Check

- "Is my information complete and accurate?"
- "What sources am I relying on, and are they reliable?"
- "Have I sought contradictory evidence?"
- "What would someone with different views see?"

### Process Check

- "Am I making this decision at the right pace?"
- "Have I considered alternatives?"
- "Am I aware of my emotional state?"
- "What's my track record on similar decisions?"

### Outcome Check

- "What are the possible outcomes, and how likely are they?"
- "What would change my mind?"
- "Am I overweighting recent events?"
- "Am I being influenced by what's easy to remember?"

## Building Bias Detection Capability

### Personal Practices

**Daily checks**:

- Start meetings by stating your hypothesis
- Ask for contrary views before sharing your view
- Test assumptions explicitly
- Note when you're feeling overconfident

**Weekly reviews**:

- Review decisions and outcomes
- Look for bias patterns
- Adjust approaches based on feedback
- Seek feedback on your biases

**Monthly deep dives**:

- Analyze significant decisions for bias
- Track your bias detection accuracy
- Update your personal bias patterns
- Refine countermeasures

---

### Team Practices

**Decision processes**:

- Require devil's advocates
- Use structured decision frameworks
- Include diverse perspectives
- Document assumptions explicitly

**Team norms**:

- Normalize questioning assumptions
- Reward surfacing contrary views
- Model intellectual humility
- Make it safe to be wrong

**Organizational practices**:

- Create feedback mechanisms
- Track decision quality
- Conduct post-mortems
- Share lessons learned

---

## Summary

Bias detection is a skill that improves with practice. Key practices:

1. **Recognize patterns**: Know the signs of common biases
2. **Use frameworks**: Apply structured checks
3. **Seek external input**: Others can see your blind spots
4. **Track your record**: Measure and improve calibration
5. **Build processes**: Structure decisions to reduce bias
6. **Stay humble**: Assume you're biased more than you think

**The goal**: Not to eliminate bias, but to recognize when it might be affecting your decisions and take appropriate countermeasures.
