# Principle Extraction Template

Principles are distilled wisdom from experience. They transform specific lessons into generalizable guidance that can be applied across situations. This template provides a structured process for extracting actionable principles from experiences.

## Purpose

This template helps you:

- Transform specific experiences into generalizable insights
- Create actionable principles from lessons learned
- Build a personal knowledge base of decision-making guidance
- Prepare for future challenges by learning from past

## The Principle Extraction Process

### Phase 1: Describe the Experience

**What happened?**

Provide a concrete description of the experience. Be specific about what occurred, who was involved, and what the outcomes were.

**Template questions**:

- What specifically happened?
- Who was involved?
- What was the context?
- What were the key events?
- What was the outcome?

**Example**:
> "Our product launch was delayed by 4 months. The original plan was to ship in April, but we didn't ship until August. The delay was caused by scope creep, underestimation of technical complexity, and team burnout from overtime."

---

### Phase 2: Understand the Outcome

**What was the result?**

Analyze the outcome objectively. Separate the facts from your interpretation.

**Template questions**:

- What were the measurable outcomes?
- What was the impact on the business?
- What was the impact on the team?
- What was the impact on customers?
- What were the unintended consequences?

**Example**:
> "The delayed launch meant we missed the seasonal buying period, resulting in 40% lower first-year revenue than projected. The team was exhausted and three key engineers left within 3 months of launch. Competitors launched similar products during our delay, reducing our first-mover advantage. However, the extra time did result in a higher quality product."

---

### Phase 3: Analyze the Decisions

**What choices were made?**

Trace back from outcome to the decisions that led there. Be honest about what was decided and why.

**Template questions**:

- What key decisions were made?
- Who made those decisions?
- What information was available at the time?
- What information was not available or ignored?
- What alternatives were considered?
- What alternatives were not considered?

**Example**:
> "Key decisions included:
> 1. Adding features to the roadmap mid-development (I agreed to this)
> 2. Not adjusting timeline when scope increased (team lead's decision)
> 3. Working overtime to meet original date instead of resetting expectations (my decision)
> 4. Not bringing in outside help when we fell behind (management decision)
>
> At the time, I thought adding features made sense because customers requested them. I didn't push back hard enough on the timeline impact. I was afraid to disappoint stakeholders by announcing a delay."

---

### Phase 4: Surface the Lessons

**What did you learn?**

Extract the specific lessons from this experience. Focus on insights that could apply broadly.

**Template questions**:

- What assumptions proved wrong?
- What did you underestimate?
- What warning signs did you ignore?
- What would you do differently?
- What should others know about this?
- What was in your control that you didn't exercise?

**Example**:
> "Key lessons:
>
> 1. **Scope changes require timeline adjustments**: Adding features doesn't cost just the feature time—it costs coordination, testing, and context-switching time.
>
> 2. **Overtime is a debt**: Working overtime to catch up means paying it back later with interest (burnout, turnover, mistakes).
>
> 3. **Early communication prevents later crisis**: Delaying bad news makes it worse. Stakeholders would rather know early and plan than be surprised.
>
> 4. **Quality and speed are not both optimizable**: We tried to do both and got neither. At some point, you have to choose.
>
> 5. **Team capacity is not linear**: Adding features doesn't mean adding time proportionally. Diminishing returns kick in quickly."

---

### Phase 5: Formulate Principles

**What guidance can you extract?**

Transform lessons into actionable principles. Principles should be specific enough to apply but general enough to transfer.

**The principle formula**:

> **When [situation], always/suspend/avoid [action], because [reasoning]**

**Components of a good principle**:

1. **Condition**: When does this apply?
2. **Action**: What should you do (or not do)?
3. **Reasoning**: Why does this work?

**Example principles**:

1. **When adding significant scope to a project, always adjust the timeline proportionally (or more), because scope changes have coordination costs beyond the feature work itself.**

2. **When a team has been working overtime for more than two weeks, suspend the current approach and reassess, because overtime creates technical debt and burnout that will be paid later.**

3. **When you know a deadline will be missed, communicate immediately, because early information enables planning while late information creates crisis.**

4. **When facing pressure to deliver both quality and speed, choose one, because trying to optimize both typically achieves neither.**

5. **When team velocity decreases despite increased effort, recognize that capacity is nonlinear, because adding work to overloaded teams decreases productivity.**

## Principle Application Framework

### Testing Principles

Before committing a principle to your toolkit, test it against other experiences:

**Template questions**:

- Does this principle match other experiences I've had?
- Is this principle falsifiable?
- What would have to be true for this principle to be wrong?
- Does this principle conflict with other principles I hold?
- What exceptions might exist?

**Example**:
> "The principle about overtime seems broadly true, but are there exceptions? Short sprints of overtime (1-2 weeks) to meet critical deadlines might be okay if followed by recovery time. Also, some high-performance cultures might sustain overtime better than others. But the underlying insight about debt and burnout seems solid."

---

### Prioritizing Principles

Not all principles are equally important. Prioritize based on:

**Impact**: How significant are the consequences when this principle is violated?

**Frequency**: How often does this situation arise?

**Generality**: How broadly does this principle apply?

**Example**:
> "The principle about scope and timeline is high impact (caused a 4-month delay), high frequency (scope creep happens constantly), and high generality (applies to projects, teams, companies). This is a core principle.
>
> The principle about overtime is medium impact, high frequency, and high generality. Also a core principle."

---

### Building Principle Stacks

Principles work together. Group related principles into stacks:

**Example stack: "Project Management"**

- When adding significant scope, always adjust timeline proportionally
- When facing pressure on quality and speed, choose one
- When team velocity decreases despite effort, reassess capacity
- When approaching 80% of estimated timeline, review and reset expectations
- When dependencies are unclear, make them explicit before committing

## Documenting Principles

### Principle Format

Use a consistent format for documenting principles:

```
**PRINCIPLE: [Name]**
**When**: [Situation trigger]
**Action**: [What to do or avoid]
**Because**: [Reasoning and evidence]
**Source**: [Experience or context]
**Status**: [Validated, Testing, Uncertain]
```

### Example Entry

```
**PRINCIPLE: Scope-Timeline Coupling**
**When**: Significant scope changes are proposed mid-project
**Action**: Adjust timeline proportionally (or more), or reject scope
**Because**: Scope changes have coordination costs beyond feature work. Each feature affects testing, integration, documentation, and context-switching. The true cost is often 2-3x the feature's apparent cost.
**Source**: Product launch delay, 2024
**Status**: Validated (3 subsequent projects confirmed)
```

## Regular Review Process

### Weekly Reflection

At the end of each week, ask:

- What decisions did I make this week?
- Did I follow my principles?
- Did I learn anything new?
- Do my principles need updating?

### Monthly Deep Dive

Each month, review:

- Significant decisions and their outcomes
- Patterns across multiple situations
- Principles that were violated or followed
- New principles to add or old ones to retire

### Quarterly Audit

Each quarter, conduct a thorough review:

- Are my principles still accurate?
- Have I been following my principles?
- What exceptions have I discovered?
- What new experiences have taught me?

## Advanced Extraction Techniques

### From Success

Don't just extract from failures. Success contains lessons too.

**Template questions for success**:

- What did we do right?
- Was success due to skill or luck?
- What could have gone wrong?
- What would we do again?
- What would we do differently?

**Example**:
> "Our successful product launch in Q3 succeeded because we:
> 1. Locked scope 6 weeks before launch
> 2. Had clear success metrics defined upfront
> 3. Prioritized ruthlessly when we fell behind
> 4. Communicated status weekly to stakeholders
>
> This confirmed several principles and taught one new one: **success metrics should be defined before launch, not after**. We would have shipped faster if we'd known what 'success' looked like earlier."

---

### From Others' Experiences

You don't need personal experience to extract principles.

**Template questions for vicarious learning**:

- What happened in this situation?
- What did this person/company do right?
- What did they do wrong?
- What can I learn without making the mistake myself?
- How does this apply to my context?

---

### From Mental Models

Principles can also be derived from applying mental models:

**Template questions**:

- What does this mental model suggest about this situation?
- What would [Model Name] say about this?
- How does this situation match or mismatch the model?
- What principle can I extract from this application?

## Common Pitfalls in Principle Extraction

### 1. Overgeneralization

**Problem**: Drawing principles from a single experience.

**Solution**: Test principles across multiple experiences before committing.

### 2. Undergeneralization

**Problem**: Creating principles too specific to apply elsewhere.

**Solution**: Extract the generalizable insight, not just the specific lesson.

### 3. Survivorship Bias

**Problem**: Only learning from successes.

**Solution**: Include failures in principle extraction.

### 4. Outcome Focus

**Problem**: Letting outcomes determine what principles seem true.

**Solution**: Judge decisions by process, not just results.

### 5. Neglecting Context

**Problem**: Principles without conditions.

**Solution**: Always specify when a principle applies.

## Application Examples

### Example 1: Career Decision

**Experience**: Turned down a job offer from a promising startup to stay at a safe corporate job. The startup became a unicorn.

**Extraction process**:

1. **What happened**: Rejected startup offer, stayed at corporate
2. **Outcome**: Startup succeeded wildly, corporate job was fine but unremarkable
3. **Decisions**: Made risk-averse choice, didn't fully weight upside potential
4. **Lessons**: Overweighted downside risk, didn't understand my own risk tolerance
5. **Principle**: When evaluating opportunity, assess expected value (probability × outcome), not just risk

---

### Example 2: Team Conflict

**Experience**: A team member was underperforming. Avoided the conversation for 6 months. The problem worsened and became a larger conflict.

**Extraction process**:

1. **What happened**: Avoided feedback conversation, problem grew
2. **Outcome**: Team morale suffered, other team members started leaving, eventual termination was messier
3. **Decisions**: Avoided hard conversation due to discomfort
4. **Lessons**: Small problems become big problems when avoided
5. **Principle**: When you notice a performance issue, have the conversation within one week, because early feedback is easier to act on and less damaging to relationships

---

### Example 3: Product Decision

**Experience**: Added a major feature against engineering's recommendation. The feature had low adoption and high maintenance cost.

**Extraction process**:

1. **What happened**: Overrode technical judgment, feature failed
2. **Outcome**: Wasted 3 months of engineering time, reduced trust with engineering team
3. **Decisions**: Made decision based on customer pressure rather than data
4. **Lessons**: Domain experts in their area should have significant weight
5. **Principle**: When technical experts recommend against a decision, get explicit evidence for your position before overriding, because experts are usually right about their domain

---

## Summary

**The principle extraction process**:

1. **Describe** the experience concretely
2. **Understand** the objective outcomes
3. **Analyze** the decisions that led there
4. **Surface** the lessons learned
5. **Formulate** actionable principles

**Key practices**:

- Extract from both successes and failures
- Test principles across experiences
- Specify conditions for when principles apply
- Document principles consistently
- Review and update regularly

**The goal**: Build a personal knowledge base of actionable wisdom that helps you make better decisions in the future.
