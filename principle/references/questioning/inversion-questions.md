# Inversion Questions

Inversion questions approach problems from unexpected angles by asking the opposite or considering failure directly. This technique, used by great thinkers from Charlie Munger to Stoic philosophers, reveals blind spots and creates more robust thinking.

## When to Use Inversion Questions

Use inversion questions when:

- Conventional thinking isn't working
- The user is stuck or going in circles
- Overconfidence might be leading astray
- Previous attempts have failed
- The approach seems obvious or easy

## The Philosophy of Inversion

Inversion is based on a powerful insight: **often we can't solve problems directly, but we can avoid them by understanding what causes failure.**

As Charlie Munger says: "Invert, always invert. Tell me where I'm going to die, that is, so I can go somewhere else."

### Why Inversion Works

1. **Creates distance**: Thinking about the opposite makes us less emotionally attached
2. **Reveals hidden assumptions**: What we assume as necessary often isn't
3. **Surfaces failure modes**: Understanding failure helps avoid it
4. **Expands the solution space**: Sometimes the answer is "don't do X"
5. **Builds humility**: Considering failure keeps us grounded

## Question Categories

### Direct Inversion

Flip the question or statement:

1. **"What if we did the opposite?"**
   - Challenge current direction
   - Example: "Instead of raising prices, what if we lowered them?"

2. **"What would failure look like?"**
   - Define the negative explicitly
   - Example: "Failure would be losing our top three customers"

3. **"What's the worst version of this decision?"**
   - Push to the extreme
   - Example: "The worst version would be spending a year on a product nobody wants"

4. **"If you had to make this fail, how would you do it?"**
   - Strategic failure analysis
   - Example: "To make this fail, I'd hire slowly, build the wrong features, and ignore customers"

5. **"What would the opposite approach look like?"**
   - Examine the mirror image
   - Example: "The opposite of 'move fast' is 'move deliberately'—what would that mean?"

### Failure Analysis Inversion

Consider how things could go wrong:

6. **"How might this fail?"**
   - Identify failure modes
   - Example: "This could fail if we can't reach the technical milestones"

7. **"What would have to go wrong for this to fail?"**
   - Stress test assumptions
   - Example: "For this to fail, we'd need key people to leave AND competitors to move fast"

8. **"What's the most likely way this doesn't work out?"**
   - Focus on probability
   - Example: "Most likely, we underestimate the time and overestimate our capabilities"

9. **"What are you most afraid of?"**
   - Surface hidden concerns
   - Example: "I'm most afraid that we'll spend 6 months on this and get nothing"

10. **"What's the mistake you're trying not to make?"**
    - Clarify the anti-goal
    - Example: "We're trying not to make the mistake of building before validating"

11. **"What does a trainwreck look like in this situation?"**
    - Paint the negative picture
    - Example: "A trainwreck would be: we miss launch, lose momentum, and key people leave"

### Avoiding Inversion

Consider what NOT to do:

12. **"What should you definitely not do?"**
    - Define clear boundaries
    - Example: "We should definitely not hire more people until we have product-market fit"

13. **"What advice would you give to someone about to make this mistake?"**
    - Use third-person wisdom
    - Example: "I'd tell them to talk to 10 customers before building anything"

14. **"What are the common ways people fail in situations like this?"**
    - Learn from others' mistakes
    - Example: "Most startups fail by running out of money before finding product-market fit"

15. **"What would you tell your past self to avoid?"**
    - Use hindsight wisdom
    - Example: "I would tell my past self to stop building and start talking to customers"

16. **"What traps should you avoid?"**
    - Name the specific hazards
    - Example: "The biggest traps are premature scaling, feature creep, and sunk cost thinking"

17. **"What are you trying not to think about?"**
    - Surface avoided topics
    - Example: "I'm avoiding thinking about whether our business model actually works"

### Contrary Position Inversion

Argue the opposite side:

18. **"What would someone who disagrees with this say?"**
    - Voice opposition
    - Example: "They'd say we're solving the wrong problem"

19. **"What are the strongest arguments against your position?"**
    - Steelman the opposition
    - Example: "The strongest argument is that we're projecting our own preferences onto users"

20. **"What would a critic say?"**
    - Channel critical perspective
    - Example: "A critic would say we're just copying what worked elsewhere"

21. **"What would your biggest skeptic be worried about?"**
    - Anticipate objections
    - Example: "My biggest skeptic would worry about our ability to execute"

22. **"If you were trying to talk someone out of this, what would you say?"**
    - Make the opposing case
    - Example: "I'd talk about the graveyard of companies that tried the same thing"

23. **"What would make this a terrible idea?"**
    - Identify dealbreakers
    - Example: "This would be terrible if we're wrong about the market size"

### Constraint Inversion

Consider limitations and impossibilities:

24. **"What if you couldn't do this?"**
    - Test necessity
    - Example: "If we couldn't raise prices, how would we improve unit economics?"

25. **"What if you had to cut this in half?"**
    - Force simplification
    - Example: "If we had to cut the team in half, who'd we keep and why?"

26. **"What if you had to stop doing this for a month?"**
    - Test dependency
    - Example: "What would break if we paused marketing for a month?"

27. **"What if you only had one week?"**
    - Force prioritization
    - Example: "If we only had one week, what would we do?"

28. **"What if you had to do this with no additional budget?"**
    - Constrain resources
    - Example: "With no additional budget, could we still make progress?"

29. **"What if you could never hire again?"**
    - Test scalability
    - Example: "If we could never hire, could we automate or eliminate anything?"

### Consequence Inversion

Think about negative outcomes:

30. **"What are the unintended consequences?"**
    - Consider side effects
    - Example: "The unintended consequence might be that we lose our early evangelists"

31. **"What would this decision cost that isn't on the balance sheet?"**
    - Account for hidden costs
    - Example: "The hidden cost is team morale when we inevitably pivot"

32. **"What would you sacrifice to achieve this?"**
    - Explicit trade-offs
    - Example: "To achieve fast growth, we'd sacrifice profitability for years"

33. **"What would you have to give up?"**
    - Face opportunity costs
    - Example: "To focus on enterprise, we'd have to neglect our SMB customers"

34. **"What would this prevent you from doing?"**
    - Consider foreclosed options
    - Example: "This partnership would prevent us from working with their competitors"

35. **"What relationships might this damage?"**
    - Think about social costs
    - Example: "This decision might damage trust with our early investors"

### Identity Inversion

Consider who you wouldn't be:

36. **"What kind of company would this make you?"**
    - Consider character implications
    - Example: "This would make us a company that prioritizes short-term over long-term"

37. **"What would you have to become to succeed here?"**
    - Examine personal change
    - Example: "I'd have to become comfortable with conflict"

38. **"What values would you have to compromise?"**
    - Face ethical trade-offs
    - Example: "To grow this fast, we'd have to compromise on hiring standards"

39. **"What would you regret in 10 years?"**
    - Apply long-term perspective
    - Example: "I'd regret not spending more time with family on this timeline"

40. **"What kind of person takes this path?"**
    - Consider narrative
    - Example: "The person who takes this path is willing to bet everything"

### Probabilistic Inversion

Consider the odds honestly:

41. **"What's the probability this actually works?"**
    - Be honest about odds
    - Example: "The probability is probably 20%, not the 80% I'm assuming"

42. **"What would 100 runs of this decision look like?"**
    - Think distribution, not just outcomes
    - Example: "In 100 runs, maybe 30 succeed and 70 fail—am I okay with that?"

43. **"What are the odds this is a mistake?"**
    - Consider base rates
    - Example: "Most pivots fail, so the base rate suggests we should be cautious"

44. **"If you were right only 30% of the time in the past, why assume you're right now?"**
    - Apply humility
    - Example: "In similar situations, I've been wrong 70% of the time"

45. **"What would happen if you tried this 10 times?"**
    - Consider variance
    - Example: "If I tried 10 times, maybe 2 would succeed by luck alone"

### Death and Mortality Inversion

Apply extreme perspective:

46. **"If this were your last decision, what would you do?"**
    - Cut to essentials
    - Example: "If this were my last decision, I'd focus on what truly matters"

47. **"If you found out in 5 years that this was the wrong call, what would you wish you'd done?"**
    - Future regret prevention
    - Example: "I'd wish I'd spent more time validating before building"

48. **"If you had one year to live, would this matter?"**
    - Apply mortality filter
    - Example: "With one year to live, I wouldn't care about this organizational issue"

49. **"What would someone at the end of their life advise?"**
    - Use wisdom of endings
    - Example: "Someone wise might say: spend time on what you love"

50. **"What do you want to be remembered for?"**
    - Consider legacy
    - Example: "I want to be remembered for building something meaningful, not just profitable"

### Solution Inversion

Find the answer by considering the anti-answer:

51. **"What do you need to avoid to succeed?"**
    - Focus on prevention
    - Example: "To succeed, I need to avoid building features nobody wants"

52. **"What does failure avoidance look like?"**
    - Make anti-goals explicit
    - Example: "Failure avoidance means: don't raise prices before value is clear"

53. **"What would you have to stop doing?"**
    - Identify cessation
    - Example: "To succeed, I'd have to stop attending every sales opportunity"

54. **"What's the opposite of the problem?"**
    - Reframe through opposition
    - Example: "Instead of 'how do we acquire customers?' ask 'how do we lose them?'"

55. **"What behaviors would guarantee failure?"**
    - Define anti-patterns
    - Example: "Guaranteed failure: ignore feedback, build in isolation, move slowly"

## Advanced Inversion Techniques

### The Pre-Mortem

As popularized by Gary Klein:

1. Imagine the project has failed spectacularly
2. Ask: "Why did it fail?"
3. Write down plausible failure reasons
4. For each reason, ask: "What can we do to prevent this?"
5. Build those preventive measures into the plan

### The Anti-Goals List

Create a list of things you explicitly won't do:

1. List 5-10 things that would definitely be mistakes
2. Review decisions against this anti-goals list
3. Use it as a constraint filter

Example:

- We won't raise prices without adding value
- We won't hire faster than we can onboard
- We won't launch before testing with real users

### The Inversion Journal

Keep a record of inversions that proved true:

- Write down your inversions
- Track which ones were accurate
- Build a personal library of wisdom

### The 2x2 Inversion Matrix

Create a matrix of what to do and what to avoid:

| DO | DON'T |
|----|-------|
| Test with users | Build without validation |
| Cut costs early | Assume revenue will cover costs |
| Communicate openly | Hide problems |
| Hire slowly | Hire for experience over fit |

## Examples in Action

### Example 1: Strategic Decision

**User**: "We should expand internationally."

**Inversion Sequence**:

- "What would the opposite decision look like?"
  - "Focus only on our home market"
- "What would the risks of international expansion be?"
  - "We'd need to localize, navigate regulations, build new relationships"
- "How might expansion fail?"
  - "We might spread too thin and lose our core market"
- "What would guarantee failure in international expansion?"
  - "Doing it all at once without learning from early markets"
- **Result**: The user decides to expand to one adjacent market instead of five

### Example 2: Product Decision

**User**: "We should add AI features to our product."

**Inversion Sequence**:

- "What would be terrible about adding AI features?"
  - "We might add complexity that confuses users"
- "What would guaranteed failure look like?"
  - "Adding features nobody asked for while ignoring existing pain points"
- "What would you avoid?"
  - "I'd avoid adding AI just because it's trendy"
- "What would you need to do to succeed with AI?"
  - "Find a specific problem AI solves better than existing solutions"
- **Result**: Instead of blanket AI features, identify one high-impact use case

### Example 3: Career Decision

**User**: "Should I accept this promotion?"

**Inversion Sequence**:

- "What would be the downside of this promotion?"
  - "More management, less hands-on work, higher expectations"
- "What would make this promotion a failure?"
  - "Taking it for the title, then failing in the new role"
- "What would your life look like if you didn't take it?"
  - "Stay technical, deeper expertise, maybe less money"
- "What would you regret either way?"
  - "I'd regret not trying, or trying and failing"
- **Result**: Clearer understanding of what success and failure look like

### Example 4: Investment Decision

**User**: "We should raise another round of funding."

**Inversion Sequence**:

- "What if you didn't raise?"
  - "We'd need to cut costs and extend runway"
- "What would be bad about taking more money?"
  - "More dilution, more pressure to grow, more expectations"
- "How might more money hurt you?"
  - "Hiring too fast, losing focus, overconfidence"
- "What does a company that raised too much fail to do?"
  - "They fail to develop discipline and focus"
- **Result**: Consider bootstrapping or smaller raise

## Best Practices for Using Inversion Questions

### Do

- **Make inversion a habit**: Before any decision, ask "what's the opposite?"
- **Be specific**: "This might fail" is less useful than "this might fail because..."
- **Use it early**: Inversion works best before commitment, not after
- **Combine with probability**: Don't just consider failure—estimate likelihood
- **Make it concrete**: Paint vivid pictures of success and failure

### Don't

- **Use inversion to avoid action**: Inversion can become procrastination
- **Focus only on negatives**: Pair inversion with clear action paths
- **Ignore the original direction**: Inversion should inform, not replace, original thinking
- **Get lost in worst-case scenarios**: Use inversion to prepare, not paralyze
- **Invert without returning**: Always bring insights back to actionable decisions

## Summary

Inversion is a powerful tool for robust thinking. By asking the opposite:

- **Obvious truths become questionable**
- **Hidden risks become visible**
- **Simplifications emerge from complexity**
- **Humility replaces overconfidence**
- **Better decisions emerge from considering failure**

Remember: Wisdom comes not from knowing what to do, but from knowing what to avoid. Invert, always invert.
