# Expansion Questions

Expansion questions broaden perspectives, reveal hidden opportunities, and help users see beyond their immediate constraints. These questions open doors rather than test them.

## When to Use Expansion Questions

Use expansion questions when:

- Thinking seems narrow or constrained
- Only one approach is being considered
- The user is focused on solving rather than discovering
- Opportunities might be hiding in plain sight
- Conventional approaches haven't worked

## The Philosophy of Expansion

Good expansion:

- **Opens new possibilities** without forcing choices
- **Respects current thinking** while extending it
- **Creates options** for later consideration
- **Builds on strengths** while surfacing alternatives
- **Expands the pie** rather than dividing it

Expansion is not about rejecting current thinking—it's about enriching it.

## Question Categories

### Expanding the Problem Space

These questions help see the problem differently:

1. **"What else is this problem like?"**
   - Draws parallels to other domains
   - Example: "This is like the classic newsvendor problem in operations research"

2. **"What problem is this actually a part of?"**
   - Places the issue in a larger context
   - Example: "High turnover is part of a larger culture problem"

3. **"If you weren't constrained by your current approach, what would you do?"**
   - Imagines the ideal solution
   - Example: "If money were no object, we'd build custom everything"

4. **"What problem would you solve if you had unlimited resources?"**
   - Reveals true priorities
   - Example: "We'd solve customer success to reduce churn entirely"

5. **"What would you do if you were starting over today?"**
   - Encourages fresh thinking
   - Example: "If I started fresh, I wouldn't build this feature at all"

6. **"What problem keeps you up at night that you haven't mentioned?"**
   - Surfaces deeper concerns
   - Example: "Actually, I'm worried about whether our tech can scale"

### Expanding the Stakeholder Map

These questions consider broader impact:

7. **"Who else should be involved in this decision?"**
   - Identifies missing voices
   - Example: "Have you talked to customers who churned?"

8. **"What would your employees say if they were in this meeting?"**
   - Considers frontline perspectives
   - Example: "They'd probably point out the operational challenges"

9. **"What would a customer think about this decision?"**
   - Puts the user in customer shoes
   - Example: "Would customers actually notice or care about this change?"

10. **"What would a skeptic say?"**
    - Voices the opposing view
    - Example: "A skeptic would ask whether we're solving symptoms, not causes"

11. **"Who benefits from the current situation?"**
    - Identifies incumbents
    - Example: "Our resellers benefit—they make money on the status quo"

12. **"Who would benefit from change?"**
    - Identifies potential allies
    - Example: "New employees would benefit from clearer processes"

13. **"What does the industry analyst think?"**
    - Brings in external perspective
    - Example: "Analysts have said this market is consolidating"

### Expanding Time Horizons

These questions consider past and future:

14. **"What can history teach us about this?"**
    - Uses historical precedent
    - Example: "Companies that survived previous downturns cut faster"

15. **"What did you learn from similar situations in the past?"**
    - Leverages personal experience
    - Example: "Last time we had this problem, we waited too long to act"

16. **"How will you feel about this decision in 5 years?"**
    - Applies the test of time
    - Example: "In 5 years, will this even matter?"

17. **"What are you optimizing for in the long run?"**
    - Balances short and long term
    - Example: "Are we making short-term gains at the expense of long-term trust?"

18. **"What would need to happen for this to matter in 10 years?"**
    - Tests strategic significance
    - Example: "For this to matter in 10 years, we need to survive the next 2"

19. **"What have others done in similar situations historically?"**
    - Studies precedent
    - Example: "Most successful companies at this stage had a different focus"

20. **"What would you tell your past self about this?"**
    - Distills lessons learned
    - Example: "I'd tell myself to validate earlier and build less"

### Expanding the Solution Space

These questions generate alternatives:

21. **"What haven't we considered yet?"**
    - Surfaces overlooked options
    - Example: "What about a partnership instead of building in-house?"

22. **"What would a completely different approach look like?"**
    - Encourages radical thinking
    - Example: "Instead of more features, what about dramatically better support?"

23. **"What would your biggest competitor do?"**
    - Uses competitive intelligence
    - Example: "They'd probably double down on their core market"

24. **"What would you advise your best friend to do?"**
    - Creates useful distance
    - Example: "I'd tell them to focus on one thing deeply"

25. **"What's the simplest version that could work?"**
    - Strips away complexity
    - Example: "Instead of a full platform, what about a single tool?"

26. **"What can you do in the next 48 hours with no additional resources?"**
    - Creates immediate options
    - Example: "We could change our messaging, improve onboarding, fix bugs"

27. **"What if you could only do one thing?"**
    - Forces prioritization
    - Example: "If I could only improve one metric, it would be activation rate"

### Expanding Understanding

These questions deepen comprehension:

28. **"What do you know now that you wish you knew then?"**
    - Extracts wisdom from experience
    - Example: "I wish I'd known how hard enterprise sales really is"

29. **"What's the mental model behind this decision?"**
    - Surfaces underlying logic
    - Example: "Are you applying a margin compression model or a growth model?"

30. **"What are the second and third-order effects?"**
    - Traces consequences further
    - Example: "If we cut prices (1st order), volume might increase (2nd), but margins compress (3rd)"

31. **"What don't you know that you need to know?"**
    - Identifies knowledge gaps
    - Example: "I don't know our true customer acquisition cost"

32. **"What's the theory behind this approach?"**
    - Examines the "why" behind the "what"
    - Example: "The theory is that network effects create defensibility"

33. **"What patterns do you see across your experiences?"**
    - Looks for themes
    - Example: "Every time we rush, we regret it"

### Expanding Context

These questions place issues in broader context:

34. **"How does this fit into larger trends?"**
    - Considers market forces
    - Example: "This is happening across the industry, not just to us"

35. **"What would the ideal world look like?"**
    - Imagines perfect conditions
    - Example: "In an ideal world, we'd have perfect information"

36. **"What constraints are you accepting that you could challenge?"**
    - Questions artificial limitations
    - Example: "We assumed we had to be profitable immediately—but do we?"

37. **"What's the question behind the question?"**
    - Goes deeper
    - Example: "Instead of 'should we pivot?' ask 'are we solving a real problem?'"

38. **"What would you do if you had to do this with half the team?"**
    - Forces creative constraints
    - Example: "With half the team, we'd have to cut three of five initiatives"

### Expanding Perspective Through Analogies

These questions use stories and analogies:

39. **"What does this remind you of from another field?"**
    - Cross-pollinates ideas
    - Example: "This is like restaurant turnover—fix the culture, not just the hiring"

40. **"What's the closest thing you've seen to this success?"**
    - Uses positive examples
    - Example: "Slack's early growth reminds me of our situation"

41. **"What famous failure is most relevant here?"**
    - Learns from others' mistakes
    - Example: "This reminds me of Kodak missing the digital camera wave"

42. **"What does this problem look like in nature?"**
    - Draws on natural metaphors
    - Example: "This is like pruning—cutting some things allows the rest to thrive"

43. **"What can you learn from companies in adjacent industries?"**
    - Expands the reference group
    - Example: "Fintech companies solved similar trust problems that we face"

### Expanding Possibility Through Constraints

These questions use constraints creatively:

44. **"What if you had to double the ambition with the same resources?"**
    - Forces breakthrough thinking
    - Example: "If we had to double growth with the same budget, what would change?"

45. **"What if you had to reduce cost by 50%?"**
    - Forces efficiency
    - Example: "A 50% cost reduction would require us to automate X and eliminate Y"

46. **"What if you could only serve one customer segment?"**
    - Forces focus
    - Example: "If we could only serve one segment, we'd choose enterprises"

47. **"What if you had to launch in half the time?"**
    - Forces prioritization
    - Example: "With half the time, we'd launch with just core features"

48. **"What if this was your last decision?"**
    - Creates urgency
    - Example: "If this were my last decision, I'd go with the more conservative option"

### Expanding Impact

These questions consider broader effects:

49. **"What's the ripple effect of this decision?"**
    - Traces consequences
    - Example: "Cutting training might save money but hurt quality and increase turnover"

50. **"What opportunities does this create?"**
    - Looks for upside
    - Example: "This pivot might open up a larger market"

51. **"What doors does this close?"**
    - Considers opportunity cost
    - Example: "Going downmarket might close the upmarket path"

52. **"What would you do with a 10x improvement in this area?"**
    - Thinks big
    - Example: "A 10x improvement in activation would change our unit economics"

53. **"How does this scale if it works?"**
    - Tests scalability
    - Example: "If this takes off, can our infrastructure handle 10x the volume?"

## Advanced Expansion Techniques

### The Oblique Strategy

Use a random or unexpected prompt to break patterns:

- Pick a random word and connect it to your problem
- Imagine you're advising a company in a different industry
- Think about what you'd do if you just won the lottery
- Consider what would happen if you had a complete monopoly

### The 10x Lens

Instead of incremental improvement, ask for 10x:

- "What would need to be true for 10x improvement?"
- "How would you approach this if you wanted 10x better, not 10% better?"
- "What would you stop doing to make room for 10x thinking?"

### The Blank Sheet

Imagine starting fresh without constraints:

1. Write down the ideal outcome
2. Work backward without constraints
3. Then ask: "What constraints are actually real?"

### The Adjacent Possible

Explore what's just beyond current reach:

- "What's one thing you could do that you're not doing?"
- "What's one market you could enter that you're not in?"
- "What's one capability you could build that you don't have?"

## Examples in Action

### Example 1: Product Strategy

**User**: "We need to add more features to compete."

**Expansion Sequence**:

- "What if you removed features instead?"
  - "That seems counterintuitive"
- "But what if your users would pay less for a simpler product?"
  - "I never thought of it that way"
- "What's the core job your product does for users?"
  - "It helps them collaborate on documents"
- "What if you did that one thing 10x better?"
  - "We could probably dominate that niche"
- **Result**: Focus might be better than features

### Example 2: Career Decision

**User**: "Should I take the job offer?"

**Expansion Sequence**:

- "What would you do if you didn't need a job?"
  - "I'd probably start my own company"
- "Why aren't you starting your own company?"
  - "Because it's risky"
- "What would make it less risky?"
  - "More savings, a co-founder, validation"
- "What could you do to work toward those things?"
  - "I could save more, network, test my idea"
- **Result**: The decision expands from "accept/reject" to "how to prepare for independence"

### Example 3: Market Entry

**User**: "We want to expand to Europe."

**Expansion Sequence**:

- "What if you started with just one country?"
  - "That's actually more manageable"
- "What if you started with just one customer in Europe?"
  - "We could learn so much from that"
- "What would that customer need to look like?"
  - "A mid-sized company with international operations"
- "Who do you know that fits that profile?"
  - "Actually, our biggest US customer has offices in London"
- **Result**: A big expansion becomes an achievable experiment

## Best Practices for Using Expansion Questions

### Do

- **Start with openness, not judgment**: Expansion is about possibilities, not correctness
- **Build on ideas**: "And what if we also..."
- **Use silence**: Give space for ideas to emerge
- **Welcome wild ideas**: First ideas often stay too conventional
- **Make connections**: Help see links between seemingly unrelated things

### Don't

- **Overwhelm with options**: A few good alternatives beat dozens of mediocre ones
- **Push too hard**: Some people need time to expand their thinking
- **Forget to return**: Bring expansion back to actionable decisions
- **Expand endlessly**: Eventually, you need to contract and decide
- **Dismiss current thinking**: Expansion adds to ideas, it doesn't replace them

## Summary

Expansion questions serve to open minds, not just solve problems. By asking the right expansion questions:

- Narrow thinking becomes broad
- Hidden opportunities become visible
- Constraints become visible or dissolve
- Creative solutions emerge
- Users develop more flexible thinking

Remember: The best solutions often come from asking "what else" until the obvious answer becomes clear—or a better one emerges.
