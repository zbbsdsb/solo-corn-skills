# Occam's Razor

## Quick Definition

When multiple explanations exist, prefer the simplest one that accounts for all the evidence.

## Full Definition

Occam's Razor is a principle of reasoning that states when faced with competing hypotheses, the one with the fewest assumptions should be selected. Named after William of Ockham, a 14th-century English friar and philosopher, the principle suggests that simplicity should be preferred in explanation-building until complexity becomes necessary. The "razor" shaves away unnecessary assumptions, cutting through the clutter of elaborate theories to reveal the core explanation. Importantly, this principle is not a law of logic but a heuristic—a practical guide that works well in most circumstances. The razor doesn't prove that simpler explanations are true, only that they should be the starting point. When evidence truly requires complex explanations, complexity should be embraced rather than artificially simplified.

## Origin & History

William of Ockham (c. 1287–1347) was an English Franciscan friar who wrote extensively on logic, natural philosophy, and theology. While he did not invent the principle—earlier thinkers including John Duns Scotus expressed similar ideas—Ockham became its most famous advocate, applying it to resolve theological and philosophical disputes. The Latin formulation "lex parsimoniae" (law of parsimony) captures the essence: entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity. The principle influenced scientific method, particularly through Isaac Newton's work, where it became embedded in the philosophy of science. Albert Einstein famously articulated a modern version: "Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler." Today, Occam's Razor guides reasoning across science, law, medicine, and everyday decision-making.

## Application Steps

1. **List all viable explanations** - Before applying the razor, ensure you have comprehensively identified possible explanations for the phenomenon.

2. **Count the assumptions for each** - For each explanation, identify and count the specific assumptions it requires to be true.

3. **Evaluate explanatory power** - Determine whether all available evidence fits each explanation equally well, or if some explanations handle evidence better.

4. **Apply the parsimony principle** - When explanations fit the evidence equally well, tentatively prefer the one requiring fewer assumptions.

5. **Accept complexity when necessary** - If the evidence genuinely requires a complex explanation, embrace complexity rather than forcing simplicity.

6. **Test predictions** - Use the chosen explanation to generate predictions and test them empirically.

7. **Remain open to revision** - New evidence may reveal that a seemingly complex explanation was actually incomplete, requiring updates.

## Real-World Examples

**Medical Diagnosis**: A patient presents with headache and fatigue. Simple explanations (stress, dehydration, lack of sleep) should be ruled out before considering complex ones (rare neurological conditions, systemic diseases). The majority of such presentations resolve with rest and hydration.

**Troubleshooting Technical Problems**: A computer won't start. The simple explanation (power cord unplugged, outlet not working) should be checked before complex ones (motherboard failure, corrupted BIOS). Technicians often find problems were caused by oversight of simple factors.

**Historical Analysis**: When explaining historical events, Occam's Razor suggests preferring explanations involving known human motivations, economics, and power dynamics over elaborate conspiracy theories requiring coordinated secret action by many people.

**Scientific Hypotheses**: When two theories explain phenomena equally well, scientists generally prefer the simpler one. Darwin's theory of natural selection was preferred over alternatives partly because it required fewer assumptions while explaining the diversity of life.

**Everyday Reasoning**: If someone doesn't respond to your message, assume they missed it or are busy before assuming they are deliberately ignoring you or have had a catastrophic event.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Premature simplification**: Applying the razor before fully understanding the phenomenon, leading to inadequate explanations.
- **Confusing simplicity with correctness**: The simplest explanation isn't always true—just more likely until evidence demands otherwise.
- **Subjective assumption counting**: Different people may count different things as "assumptions," leading to inconsistent application.
- **Ignoring complex reality**: Some phenomena genuinely are complex, and forcing simplicity leads to poor models.
- **Using simplicity as an excuse**: Sometimes people invoke Occam's Razor to avoid engaging with difficult complexities.
- **Incommensurable explanations**: Some explanations can't be directly compared for simplicity because they operate at different levels.
