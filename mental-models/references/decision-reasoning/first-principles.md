# First Principles Thinking

## Quick Definition

Breaking down complex problems into their most basic, fundamental components to rebuild understanding from the ground up.

## Full Definition

First principles thinking is a problem-solving approach that involves deconstructing any assumption, belief, or conventional wisdom to identify the fundamental truths of a situation. Rather than accepting that things are the way they are because "that's how they've always been done," this method demands that you question every element of a problem until you reach axioms that cannot be deduced any further. The core idea is that by starting from scratch with verified building blocks, you can often discover more effective solutions that would be invisible when thinking by analogy. This approach is particularly powerful when dealing with complex systems, innovative challenges, or situations where existing solutions have plateaued in effectiveness.

## Origin & History

The concept of first principles thinking traces its roots to ancient Greek philosophy, particularly Aristotle's distinction between knowing something "by coincidence" versus knowing it "by virtue of its essence." Aristotle argued that true scientific knowledge came from understanding the first causes of things. In modern times, the method gained renewed prominence through figures like Bertrand Russell in analytic philosophy and later through Elon Musk, who popularized the term in business and technology contexts. Musk has explicitly credited first principles thinking as the foundation for SpaceX's approach to rocket manufacturing—questioning why rockets cost so much by breaking down materials costs and manufacturing processes to their fundamentals rather than accepting industry assumptions about price floors.

## Application Steps

1. **Identify the current assumption or conventional solution** - State what everyone assumes to be true about the problem or situation.

2. **Question every element** - Ask "Why is this true?" for each assumption until you reach something that cannot be questioned further.

3. **Break down to fundamental truths** - Separate facts from opinions, conventions from laws of nature, and find the irreducible elements.

4. **Build new reasoning from foundations** - Using only the verified fundamental elements, reconstruct your understanding and potential solutions.

5. **Test against reality** - Validate your new approach by comparing it against observable reality and practical constraints.

6. **Iterate and refine** - Continue questioning your new conclusions to ensure they remain grounded in first principles.

## Real-World Examples

**SpaceX Rocket Development**: Instead of accepting the conventional wisdom that rockets are expensive because "that's how they are," SpaceX engineers broke down rocket costs to raw materials (aluminum, titanium, carbon fiber) and asked whether they could manufacture rockets at material cost plus reasonable margin. This first principles approach led to reusable rocket technology that dramatically reduced launch costs.

**Tesla Battery Technology**: When assessing whether affordable electric vehicles were feasible, Tesla questioned whether battery costs were fundamentally constrained by physics and materials. By analyzing the periodic table and mass production economics, they identified pathways to cost reductions that others dismissed as impossible.

**Business Strategy**: A startup entering a mature market might use first principles to question why incumbents operate the way they do—examining whether their cost structures, organizational structures, or customer assumptions are truly necessary or merely historical artifacts.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Confusing opinions with facts**: Failing to distinguish between genuinely fundamental truths and personal preferences disguised as axioms.
- **Incomplete decomposition**: Stopping too early in the breakdown process and accepting assumptions that could be further questioned.
- **Ignoring practical constraints**: Building elegant theoretical frameworks that ignore real-world limitations like physics, regulations, or resource availability.
- **Overconfidence in重建 solutions**: Believing that starting fresh always yields better results when sometimes conventional wisdom contains hard-won wisdom.
- **Analysis paralysis**: Getting so caught up in questioning everything that progress becomes impossible.
