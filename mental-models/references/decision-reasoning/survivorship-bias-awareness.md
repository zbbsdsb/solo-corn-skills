# Survivorship Bias Awareness

## Quick Definition

Understanding that focusing only on successes obscures the failures that inform realistic probability assessments.

## Full Definition

Survivorship bias is a cognitive error that occurs when we concentrate on people or things that made it past some selection process and overlook those that did not, typically because of their lack of visibility. By focusing exclusively on the survivors of a particular endeavor or situation, we systematically overestimate the probability of success and misunderstand the true characteristics of the population. This bias leads to flawed conclusions because the failed cases—which are often equally instructive or more instructive—are missing from our analysis. The classic example involves the WWII aircraft armor study, where engineers wanted to reinforce planes based on returning aircraft, but the missing returning planes (those that didn't survive) held the crucial information about where the planes were actually being hit. Understanding this bias is essential for anyone making decisions based on others' experiences.

## Origin & History

The concept gained widespread recognition through Abraham Wald's work during World War II at the Statistical Research Group (SRG). When the US Army Air Forces asked where to add armor to bombers, Wald pointed out that the data they had—bullet holes in returning planes—represented the places planes could be hit and survive. The holes in returning planes indicated where armor was NOT needed because those hits didn't bring the plane down. The missing data—planes that didn't return—showed where hits were fatal. This insight saved countless lives. The term "survivorship bias" itself emerged later as psychologists and statisticians studied errors in reasoning about past successes. Today it is recognized as one of the most prevalent and consequential biases affecting business, investment, academic, and personal decision-making.

## Application Steps

1. **Identify the visible population** - When studying any group of "successes," ask what population they were selected from.

2. **Consider the selection process** - Understand what criteria determined who or what ended up in your visible sample.

3. **Imagine the counterfactuals** - Ask "What would I see if I could observe the failures?" Specifically, what might failed attempts have looked like?

4. **Seek out failure data** - Actively look for information about failures, not just successes. This data is often less visible but more valuable.

5. **Adjust probability estimates** - When learning from successes, remember that for every visible success, there may be many invisible failures.

6. **Question "lessons from success"** - Be skeptical of advice from successful people about what causes success, unless they also study failures.

7. **Consider your own selection** - When analyzing your own situation, recognize what selection process brought you to your current position.

## Real-World Examples

**Startup Success Advice**: Successful entrepreneurs often give advice about what made them successful. But survivorship bias means we don't hear from the vast majority who followed the same advice and failed. The advice may be wrong, incomplete, or only relevant under specific conditions.

**Investment Guru Success**: Famous investors who beat the market for years attract attention and followers. Survivorship bias reminds us that for every famous successful investor, there are many who tried similar strategies and failed—often silently leaving the market.

**College Dropout Success Stories**: Media disproportionately covers dropouts who became billionaires (Bill Gates, Mark Zuckerberg). This creates survivorship bias about the value of college education, ignoring the many more who dropped out and struggled.

**Military Lessons**: After battles, commanders often study successful tactics from survivors. But the same tactics may have been used by units that were destroyed—they just aren't around to report on their failures.

**Restaurant Success**: Looking at successful restaurants to understand the restaurant business ignores the many that failed. Success often depends on factors unique to that location, timing, and operator that can't be replicated.

**Medication Effectiveness**: Early studies of a medication's effectiveness may only include patients who tolerated it long enough to show results. Patients who stopped taking it due to side effects are often not well-represented.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Studying winners only**: Analyzing successful cases without understanding the failure rate or the characteristics of failures.
- **Invisible selection**: Not recognizing that the visible population was selected through some process that removed the failures.
- **Success-story oversampling**: Overweighting memorable success stories over systematic data on base rates.
- **Misunderstanding success factors**: Attributing success to the wrong causes because you can't observe what failed cases did differently.
- **Ignoring base rates**: Not adjusting for how rare success actually is in the underlying population.
- **Success bias in career planning**: Focusing on successful people in a field without understanding how many tried and failed.
