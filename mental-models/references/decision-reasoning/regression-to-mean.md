# Regression to Mean

## Quick Definition

Extreme performance tends to be followed by more moderate performance, simply due to statistical probability.

## Full Definition

Regression to the mean is a statistical phenomenon where, after an extreme event or measurement, the next measurement is likely to be closer to the average. This occurs because extreme outcomes are often partly due to luck or random variation, which is unlikely to repeat. When an outcome is unusually good or bad, it typically reflects a combination of skill or underlying ability plus random factors. The random factors are unlikely to align the same way in subsequent measurements, so performance tends to "regress" toward the average. This principle is often misunderstood or misattributed to other causes—unusually good performance may be credited to specific actions that actually had little effect, while unusually poor performance may be blamed on correctable errors when it was just statistical noise. Understanding regression to the mean helps prevent overreaction to both good and bad outcomes.

## Origin & History

The concept was first systematically described by Sir Francis Galton in 1885, after studying the heights of parents and their children. Galton observed that tall parents tended to have children who were tall but not as tall as themselves, and short parents had children who were short but not as short—a regression toward the mean height. Galton coined the term "regression" in statistics from this observation. However, the concept was later formalized mathematically within the broader framework of statistical inference. Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky highlighted regression to the mean as a major factor in human misprediction of future performance, showing that people systematically fail to account for the role of luck in extreme outcomes. This insight is now considered essential for accurate reasoning about performance, talent identification, and causal attribution.

## Application Steps

1. **Establish the baseline** - Determine what the average or expected performance is for the phenomenon you're observing.

2. **Identify extreme performance** - Note when an outcome is significantly above or below the baseline.

3. **Consider the role of luck** - Ask what proportion of the extreme outcome might be due to factors beyond consistent skill or ability.

4. **Expect moderation** - Anticipate that the next measurement will likely be closer to average, regardless of whether the extreme was "good" or "bad."

5. **Resist causal misattribution** - Before attributing extreme performance to specific causes, consider whether random variation might explain it.

6. **Avoid overreaction** - Don't dramatically change strategies, personnel, or investments based solely on extreme outcomes.

7. **Gather more data** - The more measurements you have, the more you can distinguish real trends from statistical noise.

## Real-World Examples

**Sports Performance**: A basketball player who shoots exceptionally well in one game is likely to shoot somewhat worse in the next game—not because they are lazy or lucky, but because their extraordinary first-game performance likely included factors that won't repeat. Similarly, a terrible shooting night doesn't indicate a permanent decline.

**Employee Performance Reviews**: An employee who has an exceptionally bad quarter may not need corrective action—they may have simply had an unlucky streak. Praising an employee excessively after one exceptional quarter is equally problematic.

**Investment Returns**: A fund that significantly outperforms the market one year is likely to underperform the following year—not because managers changed, but because exceptional performance often includes market conditions that won't repeat. This is why past performance is a poor predictor of future returns.

**Medical Observations**: A patient with an unusually high blood pressure reading at one visit will often have a lower reading at the next visit—not because of treatment administered after the first reading, but because of natural variation. This is why multiple measurements are used for diagnosis.

**Academic Performance**: A student who scores far above average on one exam is likely to score somewhat lower on the next exam—not because they are slacking off, but because their first score included some performance elements that won't repeat.

**Weather Patterns**: An unusually hot day in a normally temperate climate is often followed by cooler weather, not because of any intervention but because extreme deviations tend to be partially corrected by natural variation.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Misattribution to intervention**: Crediting improvement after poor performance to corrective actions when regression to mean would have occurred anyway.
- **Misattribution to punishment**: Blaming decline after good performance on criticism or management actions when regression alone explains it.
- **Small sample overconfidence**: Drawing conclusions from one or two measurements rather than waiting for larger samples.
- **Forgetting base rates**: Not considering how extreme an outcome is relative to the normal distribution.
- **Regression fallacy**: Assuming that regression to mean is the explanation when there might be a real causal factor.
- **Expecting permanence**: Expecting extreme performance to continue indefinitely without understanding its statistical improbability.
