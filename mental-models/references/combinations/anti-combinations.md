# Mental Model Anti-Combinations

Documented cases where mental models conflict, interfere, or should not be applied together. Understanding these anti-patterns prevents analytical confusion and decision paralysis.

---

## Fundamental Conflicts

### 1. Hanlon's Razor vs. Authority Bias Awareness

**The Conflict:** Hanlon's Razor assumes incompetence over malice; Authority Bias Awareness assumes authority claims deserve scrutiny.

**Why They Clash:**
- Hanlon's attributes negative outcomes to honest mistakes
- Authority Bias Awareness questions whether authority figures can be trusted
- Together, they create interpretive paralysis: should you assume the authority figure is incompetent or malicious?

**When This Matters:**
- Evaluating feedback from supervisors or experts
- Interpreting institutional decisions
- Assessing blame in organizational failures

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Apply Authority Bias Awareness first to establish credibility baseline
2. Only if credibility is confirmed, apply Hanlon's to interpret behavior
3. If malice is actually suspected, investigate formally rather than assuming incompetence

**Do Not Apply:**
- Both models simultaneously when assigning blame
- Hanlon's to explain away credible concerns
- Authority Bias Awareness to dismiss legitimate authority opinions

---

### 2. Commitment Consistency vs. Confirmation Bias Awareness

**The Conflict:** Commitment Consistency leverages prior stances to shape current behavior; Confirmation Bias Awareness seeks disconfirming evidence for prior beliefs.

**Why They Clash:**
- Commitment Consistency reinforces existing positions
- Confirmation Bias Awareness seeks to break reinforcement
- Together, they create action paralysis: should you honor your commitment or actively seek evidence against it?

**When This Matters:**
- Re-evaluating past strategic decisions
- Updating beliefs after new information
- Adapting to changed circumstances

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Separate the decision context: past commitment evaluation vs. new information integration
2. Use Confirmation Bias Awareness when evaluating evidence quality
3. Use Commitment Consistency when deciding whether to honor explicit promises
4. When both seem relevant, distinguish between belief updating and behavior consistency

**Do Not Apply:**
- Confirmation Bias Awareness to justify abandoning legitimate commitments
- Commitment Consistency to resist updating beliefs that need revision
- Both simultaneously when forming new commitments

---

### 3. First Principles vs. Occam's Razor (Premature Application)

**The Conflict:** First Principles deconstructs problems to find fundamental elements; Occam's Razor simplifies to the simplest explanation.

**Why They Clash:**
- First Principles often reveals that "simple" solutions miss critical complexity
- Occam's Razor applied too early eliminates important nuances found through principled analysis
- Together, they create premature simplification: the razor's simplicity cuts away what first principles just revealed

**When This Matters:**
- Problem definition phase
- Innovation and solution design
- Understanding complex systems

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Complete First Principles deconstruction before applying Occam's Razor
2. Apply Occam's Razor only after fundamental elements are identified
3. When in doubt, favor First Principles over Occam's in novel or complex situations

**Do Not Apply:**
- Occam's Razor during problem framing
- Both simultaneously when exploring new domains
- First Principles and then immediately simplifying away the discoveries

---

### 4. Asymmetric Betting vs. Regression to Mean (Timing Conflict)

**The Conflict:** Asymmetric Betting positions for extreme outcomes; Regression to Mean expects extremes to normalize.

**Why They Clash:**
- Asymmetric bets require extreme outcomes to materialize
- Regression to Mean suggests extremes are unlikely to persist
- Together, they create positioning confusion: are you betting for or against mean reversion?

**When This Matters:**
- Position sizing decisions
- Investment allocation
- Risk-reward calibration

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Use Regression to Mean to set baseline expectations
2. Use Asymmetric Betting to position for cases where regression is delayed or overwhelmed
3. Distinguish between betting WITH regression (contrarian positioning) and betting AGAINST regression (momentum positioning)

**Do Not Apply:**
- Asymmetric Betting without first establishing regression expectations
- Regression to Mean as the sole basis for position sizing
- Both simultaneously without clarifying which direction the asymmetry runs

---

### 5. OODA Loop vs. Waterfall Planning Mentality

**The Conflict:** OODA Loop emphasizes speed and iteration; Waterfall-style planning assumes comprehensive upfront analysis is possible.

**Why They Clash:**
- OODA Loop cycles quickly through Observe-Orient-Decide-Act
- Waterfall planning requires complete analysis before any action
- Together, they create execution paralysis: do you act quickly or plan completely?

**When This Matters:**
- Rapidly evolving situations
- Innovation projects
- Crisis response

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Use Two Boxes Thinking to separate what can be planned vs. what requires adaptation
2. Apply OODA Loop for the uncertain, changing elements
3. Reserve thorough planning only for stable, well-understood components
4. Default to OODA Loop in ambiguous situations

**Do Not Apply:**
- OODA Loop thinking to components that genuinely require comprehensive planning
- Waterfall planning to situations that require rapid adaptation
- Both together by attempting to plan all OODA Loop iterations upfront

## Context-Specific Anti-Combinations

### High Stakes Decision Context

**Anti-Combination:** Margin of Safety + Margin of Safety (Excessive)

**The Problem:** Applying excessive safety margins to everything creates organizational paralysis. Not every decision requires maximum buffer.

**Why It Happens:**
- Risk aversion becomes self-reinforcing
- Safety margins compound across many decisions
- The organization stops taking necessary risks

**When This Matters:**
- Portfolio management with excessive hedging
- Career decisions with overprotected options
- Business development with zero-risk requirements

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Apply Two Boxes Thinking to separate high-stakes from low-stakes decisions
2. Use asymmetric betting to concentrate safety margins where stakes are genuinely high
3. Accept lower margins in areas where failure is recoverable

---

### Personal Development Context

**Anti-Combination:** Circle of Competence + Control Illusions

**The Problem:** Expanding one's Circle of Competence requires leaving the circle—attempting things outside current capability.

**Why It Happens:**
- Circle of Competence defines reliable expertise
- Growth requires operating outside that reliability
- Combined, they justify staying inside the circle indefinitely

**When This Matters:**
- Career transitions
- Skill development
- Entrepreneurship

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Use Circle of Competence to identify expansion targets
2. Apply Two Boxes Thinking to separate controllable growth elements from uncontrollable market factors
3. Use Optionality to preserve escape routes while expanding
4. Accept temporary incompetence as necessary cost of growth

---

### System Intervention Context

**Anti-Combination:** Leverage Points + Component-Level Optimization

**The Problem:** Leverage Points exist precisely where component optimization fails; applying component thinking to leverage points misses the intervention opportunity.

**Why It Happens:**
- Leverage Points often involve counterintuitive relationships
- Component optimization works at lower system levels
- Combined, they optimize the wrong variables systematically

**When This Matters:**
- Organizational change initiatives
- Policy design
- Market interventions

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Use Emergence to confirm you're working at the right system level
2. Apply Leverage Points analysis BEFORE component optimization
3. Verify that component improvements actually impact system-level outcomes

## Situational Anti-Combinations

### When You Have Complete Information

**Anti-Combination:** Bayesian Thinking + Uncertainty Framing

**The Problem:** When information is complete, Bayesian updating adds unnecessary complexity. Uncertainty models are for genuinely uncertain situations.

**Why It Happens:**
- Bayesian Thinking provides a framework for belief updating
- Some decisions have fully known consequences
- Applying uncertainty frameworks to certain situations creates false complexity

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Confirm information completeness before choosing analytical framework
2. Apply deterministic models when all variables are known
3. Reserve Bayesian Thinking for genuine partial information

---

### When You Have Unlimited Time

**Anti-Combination:** OODA Loop + Comprehensive Analysis

**The Problem:** OODA Loop optimizes for speed; comprehensive analysis requires time. Forcing both together produces neither speed nor depth.

**Why It Happens:**
- OODA Loop was designed for time-pressured decisions
- Some situations genuinely allow thorough analysis
- Applying speed frameworks to unhurried contexts reduces analytical quality

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Assess time constraints before choosing framework intensity
2. For genuinely unhurried decisions, allow comprehensive analysis
3. Reserve OODA Loop for situations where speed actually matters

---

### When Dealing with Altruistic Actors

**Anti-Combination:** Game Theory + Incentive Structures (Pure Self-Interest Assumption)

**The Problem:** Game Theory and Incentive Structures assume self-interested actors. Applying them to genuinely altruistic or principled actors produces systematically wrong predictions.

**Why It Happens:**
- Both models predict behavior from self-interest
- Some actors genuinely operate from other motivations
- The models work so well on self-interested actors that they get overapplied

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Assess actor motivations before applying strategic models
2. Apply Game Theory and Incentive Structures to self-interested actors
3. Use different models (values-based analysis) for principled actors
4. Default to strategic models when motivation is uncertain

---

### When Past Data Is Unrepresentative

**Anti-Combination:** Learning Curve + Historical Performance Extrapolation

**The Problem:** Learning Curve assumes improvement follows predictable patterns based on practice. If the environment has changed, historical learning rates don't apply.

**Why It Happens:**
- Learning Curve provides a useful improvement expectation framework
- Past data often reflects past conditions
- Combining them extrapolates learning into environments where it won't occur

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Assess environmental stability before applying historical learning curves
2. If environment has changed significantly, expect different learning dynamics
3. Use First Principles to understand new learning requirements

## Analytical Paralysis Patterns

### The Uncertainty Spiral

**Models Involved:** Optionality + Asymmetric Betting + Bayesian Thinking + Margin of Safety

**The Problem:** Each model adds a layer of protection against being wrong. Applied together, they prevent any decisive action.

**Symptoms:**
- Endless option preservation
- Position sizes too small to matter
- Updating beliefs indefinitely without acting

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Set decision deadlines
2. Define minimum viable position/action
3. Accept that some uncertainty must be tolerated to act

---

### The Complexity Trap

**Models Involved:** Feedback Loops + Emergence + Chaos Theory + Tipping Points

**The Problem:** Each model reveals additional complexity. Applied together, they justify never acting because you can always see more complexity to explore.

**Symptoms:**
- Analysis never complete
- Interventions repeatedly delayed
- The system is "too complex to predict"

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Accept that all models are simplifications
2. Set analysis completion criteria
3. Recognize that inaction is also a choice with consequences

---

### The Consistency Lock

**Models Involved:** Commitment Consistency + Authority Bias + Incentive Structures

**The Problem:** Each model reinforces existing positions from different angles. Applied together, they lock in past decisions regardless of new evidence.

**Symptoms:**
- Past commitments never revisited
- Authority opinions treated as conclusive
- Incentives seen as the only explanation for behavior

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Schedule regular commitment review periods
2. Apply Confirmation Bias Awareness to check whether you're defending rather than evaluating
3. Distinguish between honoring commitments and avoiding necessary change

## Application Anti-Patterns

### 1. The Comprehensive Coverage Trap

**Models Attempted:** All available models applied simultaneously

**The Problem:** Each model offers genuine insight; applying all simultaneously creates contradiction and cognitive overload.

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Limit active models to 4-6 per decision
2. Use category-level selection rather than comprehensive coverage
3. Accept that some valid considerations will be excluded

### 2. The Framework Shopping Trap

**Models Attempted:** Switching between models without commitment

**The Problem:** Each model suggests different conclusions; switching between them prevents any conclusion from forming.

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Select a model framework before analysis begins
2. Complete analysis with selected framework before switching
3. Use switching to compare frameworks, not to avoid commitment

### 3. The Theoretical Purity Trap

**Models Attempted:** Perfect application of ideal model to imperfect context

**The Problem:** Models are simplifications; forcing perfect theoretical application to messy reality produces worse outcomes than imperfect application.

**Resolution Approach:**
1. Accept model approximations as features, not bugs
2. Adapt model application to context realities
3. Prefer "good enough" model application over "perfect" misapplication

## Recovery Protocols

### When You've Applied a Conflicting Combination

1. **Identify the conflict** - which models are pulling in different directions?
2. **Return to the decision context** - what situation type are you in?
3. **Apply context-specific resolution** from the relevant section above
4. **Document the conflict** for future reference
5. **Make the decision** even if imperfect - indecision is also a choice

### When You're Unsure Which Model to Apply

1. **Default to the simpler model** when complexity isn't warranted
2. **Default to the faster model** when time pressure exists
3. **Default to the model with better feedback loops** when you can learn from outcomes
4. **Accept that some decisions don't require models** - experience and judgment suffice

## Principles for Avoiding Anti-Combinations

1. **Match model complexity to situation complexity** - simple situations don't need complex frameworks
2. **Respect model assumptions** - models work within their assumptions; violating assumptions breaks predictions
3. **Limit active models per decision** - 4-6 focused models outperform 12 confused ones
4. **Default to action** - indecision has costs; models should enable better decisions, not prevent them
5. **Accept trade-offs** - choosing one model means deprioritizing another; perfection in coverage is impossible
6. **Update based on outcomes** - when combinations fail, update your understanding of what works
