# Anchoring Bias

## Quick Definition

The tendency to rely heavily on the first piece of information encountered (the anchor) when making decisions, even when that information is irrelevant or arbitrary.

## Full Definition

Anchoring bias is a cognitive bias where individuals depend too heavily on an initial piece of information offered (considered to be the "anchor") when making subsequent judgments or decisions. Once an anchor is set, other estimates are adjusted toward the anchor, and people fail to adjust sufficiently away from it even when they know the anchor is irrelevant or arbitrary.

This bias operates through several mechanisms. First, initial information creates a reference point that shapes subsequent judgments. Second, cognitive constraints make it difficult to "anchor" on no information or to consider all available information equally. Third, the availability heuristic intersects with anchoring—the first piece of information tends to be most available in memory. Fourth, once committed to an anchor, people seek information consistent with it rather than information that might challenge it.

The power of anchoring is demonstrated by the fact that even obviously arbitrary anchors influence judgments. In classic experiments, participants who were first asked whether Gandhi was older or younger than 114 years gave much higher age estimates than those asked about 35 years—despite knowing that 114 was absurd. The arbitrary anchor pulled estimates upward regardless of its absurdity.

Anchoring effects appear across many domains: salary negotiations (first offer sets the range), real estate (listing price influences final price), pricing (original price makes sale price seem good), judicial sentencing (initial proposals anchor final decisions), and general estimation tasks where any numerical starting point influences final estimates.

Understanding anchoring is essential for negotiators who must avoid being anchored disadvantageously, for consumers making purchase decisions, for professionals making estimates, and for anyone trying to make unbiased judgments in quantitative domains.

## Origin & History

Anchoring bias was first systematically documented by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in their groundbreaking 1974 paper "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." Their research demonstrated that numerical estimates were systematically influenced by initial values, even when those values were clearly random or irrelevant to the task at hand.

One of their most famous experiments involved a wheel of fortune rigged to stop at either 10 or 65. Participants were asked whether the percentage of African nations in the United Nations was higher or lower than the number shown, then to estimate the actual percentage. Those who saw 10 estimated an average of 25%, while those who saw 65 estimated 45%—a 20-point difference attributable entirely to an arbitrary wheel spin.

The robustness of anchoring effects led Tversky and Kahneman to conclude that the bias operates through insufficient adjustment rather than through initial judgment alone. People start at the anchor and move in the direction indicated, but don't move far enough.

Subsequent research has explored anchoring in professional domains. Dan Ariely and colleagues demonstrated dramatic anchoring effects in negotiations, showing that arbitrary first offers (like Social Security numbers used as price anchors for consumer electronics) strongly influenced final deal prices. Other research has examined anchoring in medical diagnosis (first test results anchor subsequent interpretation), legal judgment (initial settlement offers anchor final outcomes), and real estate (list prices anchor final sale prices).

Contemporary research continues to examine the neural basis of anchoring, the moderators of anchoring effects, and strategies for debiasing. Studies using neuroimaging have identified brain regions involved in anchor processing, while field studies have documented anchoring in professional practice.

## Application Steps

**Step 1: Be Aware of First Information Power**
Recognize that the first numerical information you encounter will likely serve as an anchor, regardless of its relevance or accuracy. This awareness is the first step toward mitigation.

**Step 2: Use Independent Reasoning First**
When facing a quantitative judgment, develop your own estimate before exposing yourself to others' numbers, market prices, or expert opinions. Your independent estimate becomes a personal anchor.

**Step 3: Consider Multiple Anchors**
Deliberately expose yourself to different starting points and compare how your judgments shift. Understanding that estimates are anchor-dependent helps reduce over-reliance on any single anchor.

**Step 4: Use Extreme-Anchor Consideration**
When evaluating a claim or offer, deliberately consider what would be true if the anchor were much higher or lower. This "consider the opposite" strategy helps reduce anchor effects.

**Step 5: Ground Estimates in Base Rates**
Anchor your reasoning in relevant statistical base rates rather than arbitrary starting points. External benchmarks provide more reliable reference points.

**Step 6: Separate Your Analysis from Others'**
In collaborative settings, have each party develop independent estimates before sharing. This prevents the first shared number from anchoring the discussion.

**Step 7: Recognize When You Are Being Anchored**
In negotiations and sales contexts, be aware that the other party's initial offer or stated position is designed to anchor you. Prepare your own anchor based on objective criteria.

**Step 8: Build in Deliberate Delays**
When making quantitative decisions, impose delays that allow you to revisit judgments made under anchor influence. Initial estimates are more anchored than revised estimates.

## Real-World Examples

**Example 1: Salary Negotiations**
A candidate applying for a position receives information about the salary range or first offer. This number becomes an anchor that shapes subsequent negotiation, even if the range was set arbitrarily or strategically. Knowing the candidate's anchor, employers often position their initial offer at the low end, expecting negotiation to move toward the middle.

**Example 2: Real Estate Pricing**
The listing price of a home serves as a powerful anchor for final sale price. Homes priced above comparable sales still tend to sell for more than they would have if priced at market value. Similarly, buyers anchor on listing prices and negotiate relative to them rather than relative to objective values.

**Example 3: Restaurant Menu Pricing**
High-priced items on restaurant menus serve as anchors that make mid-priced items seem reasonable by comparison. Even if the expensive items are rarely ordered, their presence increases perception of value for the "recommended" items.

**Example 4: Negotiation Dynamics**
In multi-issue negotiations, the first number mentioned often strongly influences the final outcome range. This creates strategic incentives to make first offers, but also strategic incentives to avoid being first when you lack good anchor information.

**Example 5: Insurance Valuations**
When insuring valuables, initial declared values become anchors that influence replacement valuations. This can lead to over-insurance (premiums exceed value) or under-insurance (settlements based on understated anchors).

**Example 6: Medical Reference Ranges**
The first diagnosis or test result often anchors subsequent medical thinking. A patient initially diagnosed with a mild condition may have serious symptoms discounted as anxiety-related, while initial serious diagnoses may lead to over-treatment of minor subsequent complaints.

**Example 7: Judicial Settlements**
In civil litigation, the initial settlement demand or offer creates an anchor that influences the settlement range. This effect persists even when the initial number was inflated or deflated for strategic purposes.

**Example 8: Product Pricing and Markdowns**
Original prices create anchors against which sale prices are evaluated. A product "reduced from $100 to $60" seems like a better deal than the identical product priced at $60 without an original price, even though the original price was likely inflated to create the anchor.

## Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Arbitrary Anchors Dominate**
Even when people know that an anchor is random or arbitrary, it still influences their estimates. Knowledge of the bias is insufficient to overcome it without deliberate strategies.

**Pitfall 2: Anchor Contamination**
When an anchor is even partially related to the question, the anchor effect strengthens and becomes more resistant to correction. Partial relevance is more dangerous than complete irrelevance.

**Pitfall 3: Anchoring on Memory vs. Environment**
People anchor on what they remember as well as what they see. Past prices, historical data, and remembered estimates all serve as anchors even when more current information is available.

**Pitfall 4: Expert Anchoring**
Professionals who should know better are not immune to anchoring effects. Studies show that experienced negotiators, real estate professionals, and financial analysts all show significant anchoring effects in their domains.

**Pitfall 5: Sequential Anchoring**
Each piece of numerical information encountered serves as a potential anchor. In extended negotiations or analyses, later anchors can override earlier ones, creating unstable and inconsistent judgments.

**Pitfall 6: Anchoring on Your Own Estimates**
Once you've made an estimate, it becomes an anchor for subsequent refinement. Initial estimates are often under-informed, yet they persist in memory as anchors for later adjustments.

**Pitfall 7: Collective Anchoring**
Group discussions tend to anchor on the first stated opinion or estimate. Particularly when stated by someone perceived as knowledgeable, the initial contribution dominates subsequent group thinking.

### Debiasing Strategies

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|----------------|
| **Independent Estimation** | Develop own estimate before exposure to others' numbers |
| **Consider the Opposite** | Deliberately consider much higher and lower anchors |
| **Base Rate Anchoring** | Ground estimates in relevant statistics |
| **Delayed Judgment** | Impose time delays between anchor exposure and estimation |
| **Multiple Anchor Consideration** | Expose yourself to several different anchors before deciding |
| **External Benchmarking** | Use objective external standards rather than any single number |
| **Precommitment** | Pre-commit to estimation processes before seeing anchors |
