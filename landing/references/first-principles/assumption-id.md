# First Principles: Assumption Identification

## What We Think We Know

**Core Question**: What are the fundamental beliefs we hold without questioning?

### Individual Assumption Checklist

- [ ] **About the problem itself**:
  - The problem is real and worth solving
  - The problem is correctly defined
  - The problem scope is appropriate
  - The problem has not already been solved

- [ ] **About the solution space**:
  - My approach is technically feasible
  - The constraints I've identified are true constraints
  - The resources needed are accurately estimated
  - The timeline assumptions are realistic

- [ ] **About the users**:
  - Users actually have this problem
  - Users want this problem solved in this way
  - Users will adopt this solution
  - User behavior predictions are accurate

- [ ] **About the context**:
  - The market conditions I've assumed are correct
  - The competitive landscape is as I perceive
  - Regulatory/legal assumptions are valid
  - Technology assumptions will hold true

## Assumption Classification Framework

### Explicit Assumptions (Stated)
> Assumptions we are aware of and have documented

| Category | Example | Confidence Level |
|----------|---------|------------------|
| Market assumptions | "Users will pay $X for this" | High/Medium/Low |
| Technical assumptions | "We can achieve X performance" | High/Medium/Low |
| Behavioral assumptions | "Users will change their behavior" | High/Medium/Low |
| Resource assumptions | "We can acquire X resources" | High/Medium/Low |

### Implicit Assumptions (Hidden)
> Assumptions we don't realize we're making

**Common Implicit Assumptions**:

1. **The way it's always been done is the only way**
   - Challenge: Has this approach ever been truly questioned?

2. **Our user's mental model matches ours**
   - Challenge: Have we validated their actual thinking process?

3. **Past success guarantees future success**
   - Challenge: Are the conditions truly identical?

4. **More features = more value**
   - Challenge: Have we measured actual utility?

5. **The constraint is real, not self-imposed**
   - Challenge: Who created this limitation and why?

6. **We understand why things work**
   - Challenge: Have we traced causality or just correlation?

## Assumption Source Analysis

### Where Assumptions Come From

| Source | Description | Risk Level |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Industry norms** | "Everyone does it this way" | High |
| **Personal experience** | "In my experience..." | Medium |
| **Expert opinion** | "The expert said..." | Medium |
| **Past success** | "It worked before" | Medium |
| **Authority figures** | "Management decided" | High |
| **Logical reasoning** | "It seems obvious that..." | Medium |
| **Cultural beliefs** | "This is how we do things" | High |
| **Mathematical proof** | Derived from first principles | Low |

### Assumption Audit Process

```
Step 1: List everything you "know" to be true
    ↓
Step 2: Categorize each as explicit or implicit
    ↓
Step 3: Trace each to its source
    ↓
Step 4: Assess confidence level (1-10)
    ↓
Step 5: Identify which assumptions, if wrong, would be most damaging
    ↓
Step 6: Design experiments to validate highest-impact assumptions
```

## The First Principles Reboot

### Destroy and Rebuild Protocol

**Question Every "Obvious" Statement**:

| Instead of asking... | Ask... |
|---------------------|---------|
| "How do we improve X?" | "Why does X exist at all?" |
| "How do we do Y better?" | "Is Y even the right approach?" |
| "What's the best solution?" | "What are we really trying to achieve?" |
| "What worked before?" | "What assumptions made it work?" |

## Next Steps

After completing this template:

1. Review your assumption list with fresh eyes in 24 hours
2. Identify your highest-risk assumptions (those with lowest confidence)
3. Design validation experiments for top 3 assumptions
4. Schedule assumption reviews at project milestones

*Remember: The goal is not to eliminate all assumptions—it's to make them conscious, validate the critical ones, and be prepared to pivot when you're wrong.*
