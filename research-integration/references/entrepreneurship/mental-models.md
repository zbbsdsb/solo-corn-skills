# Mental Models in Entrepreneurship

This document extracts 5 core mental models from the foundational literature in entrepreneurship, covering the entire entrepreneurial process from opportunity identification to large-scale growth. Each model can be used independently or in combination.

---

## Lean Canvas

**Source**
Ash Maurya (2012). *Running Lean: How to Iterate from Plan A to a Plan That Works*. O'Reilly Media.
Inspired by Eric Ries (2011). *The Lean Startup*.

### Definition
The Lean Canvas is a single-page business analysis tool designed for entrepreneurial projects, focusing on solving the problem of validating business assumptions in highly uncertain environments. It compresses complex entrepreneurial plans into 9 core building blocks, helping entrepreneurs quickly identify the riskiest key assumptions and prioritize testing and verification.

### Visual Structure
```
┌─────────────┬─────────────┬─────────────┐
│   Problem   │  Solution   │  Unique     │
│  Top 3      │  Top 3      │  Value      │
│             │             │  Proposition│
├─────────────┼─────────────┼─────────────┤
│  Key Metrics│  Unfair     │  Channels   │
│             │  Advantage  │             │
├─────────────┴─────────────┴─────────────┤
│           Cost Structure  Revenue Model  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Application Steps
1. **Identify Problems**: List the Top 3 problems faced by target customers, scoring by "severity × frequency"
2. **Define Customer Profile**: Clarify the specific characteristics of early adopters
3. **Build Solutions**: Propose Top 3 solutions for each problem
4. **Validate Unique Value Proposition**: Ensure the differentiation advantage of the solution
5. **Identify Key Metrics**: Define North Star Metrics for measuring success
6. **Plan Channel Strategy**: Design minimum viable acquisition channels
7. **Set Cost Structure**: Estimate fixed costs and marginal costs
8. **Design Revenue Model**: Clarify pricing and charging models
9. **Extract Unfair Advantage**: Identify non-easily replicable moats

### Case
Dropbox used the Lean Canvas in its early days to validate the demand for file synchronization. Founder Drew Houston identified "cross-device file synchronization" as users' Top 1 problem rather than storage space. The MVP validated market demand through a 4-minute video demonstration rather than a complete product, reducing development risk.

### Combination Tips
- **+ Build-Measure-Learn Loop**: Use the Lean Canvas to define assumptions, use the feedback loop to validate assumptions
- **+ Crossing the Chasm**: Use the Lean Canvas to analyze niche market characteristics when selecting a beachhead market
- **+ Business Model Canvas**: Transition from the Lean Canvas to the Business Model Canvas to prepare for scaling

## Business Model Canvas

**Source**
Osterwalder, A., & Pigneur, Y. (2010). *Business Model Generation*. Wiley.

### Definition
The Business Model Canvas is a systematic framework describing how an enterprise creates, delivers, and captures value, decomposing a business model into 9 interrelated building blocks. It provides a "business language" shared by entrepreneurs and enterprise managers, making the design, discussion, and iteration of business models visual.

### Visual Structure
```
                    ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
                    │        Value Proposition          │
                    │   What problem do you solve/      │
                    │   what need do you meet?          │
  ┌───────────────┐ └──────────────────────────────────┘
  │ Customer      │         ┌───────────────┐
  │ Segments      │ ─────── │  Channels     │
  │ Who do you    │         │               │
  │ serve?        │         └───────────────┘
  └───────────────┘
                    ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
  ┌───────────────┐ │       Customer Relationships     │
  │ Key Resources │─┼── Key Activities                 │
  │               │ │                                  │
  └───────────────┘ └──────────────────────────────────┘
                    │        Cost Structure   Revenue   │
                    │                           Streams │
                    └──────────────────────────────────┘
                    └── Key Partnerships ───────────────
```

### Application Steps
1. **Customer Segments**: Define the different user groups you want to serve
2. **Value Propositions**: Clarify the unique value combinations that help customers solve problems or meet needs
3. **Channels**: Describe how to reach customers and deliver value propositions
4. **Customer Relationships**: Define the ways to establish and maintain customer relationships
5. **Revenue Streams**: Reveal how to obtain cash from each customer group
6. **Key Resources**: List the key assets required to implement the business model
7. **Key Activities**: Describe the most important activities that must be performed to create and deliver value propositions
8. **Key Partnerships**: Describe the network of suppliers and other partners
9. **Cost Structure**: Cover all costs incurred in operating the business model

### Case
Airbnb's Business Model Canvas analysis:
- **Customer Segments**: Travelers (looking for unique accommodation experiences), Hosts (people with vacant properties)
- **Value Propositions**: Unique accommodation experiences + community trust + cost-effectiveness
- **Key Resources**: Two-sided user network, brand, technical platform
- **Key Activities**: Platform operation, trust system establishment, quality control
- **Revenue Streams**: 3-12% commission on each transaction

### Combination Tips
- **+ Lean Canvas**: The Lean Canvas is suitable for early rapid validation, the Business Model Canvas is suitable for complete planning before scaling
- **+ Effectuation**: Design business models starting from "Bird-in-Hand" rather than from perfect plans
- **+ Disruptive Innovation**: Use the Business Model Canvas to analyze the business model weaknesses of large enterprises and find disruption opportunities

## Build-Measure-Learn Loop

**Source**
Ries, E. (2011). *The Lean Startup: How Today's Entrepreneurs Use Continuous Innovation to Create Radically Successful Businesses*. Crown Business.

### Definition
The Build-Measure-Learn Loop is the core feedback mechanism of the Lean Startup, defining the entrepreneurial process as a continuous experimental cycle: transforming valuable ideas into a Minimum Viable Product (MVP), measuring user reactions through quantitative indicators, extracting learning from successes and failures, and then generating new assumptions to enter the next cycle. The faster the cycle speed, the higher the iteration efficiency.

### Visual Structure
```
         ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐
         │                                        │
         │    ┌───────────────┐                   │
         │    │   Ideas/      │                   │
         │    │  Assumptions  │                   │
         │    └───────┬───────┘                   │
         │            │                           │
         │            ▼                           │
         │    ┌───────────────┐                   │
         │    │    Build      │                   │
         │    │    (MVP)      │                   │
         │    └───────┬───────┘                   │
         │            │                           │
         │            ▼                           │
         │    ┌───────────────┐                   │
         │    │   Measure     │                   │
         │    │               │                   │
         │    └───────┬───────┘                   │
         │            │                           │
         │            ▼                           │
         │    ┌───────────────┐                   │
         │    │    Learn      │                   │
         │    └───────┬───────┘                   │
         │            │                           │
         │            └───────────────────────────┘
         │                  Feedback Loop          │
         │           Valid → Amplify               │
         │           Invalid → Pivot               │
         └────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Application Steps
1. **Clarify Ideas**: Identify core business assumptions that need to be verified
2. **Build MVP**: Develop a Minimum Viable Product to quickly reach real users
3. **Measure Reactions**: Track user behavior and feedback using quantitative indicators
4. **Extract Learning**: Analyze data to verify whether assumptions hold
5. **Decide to Pivot**: Choose to persevere or adjust direction based on results
6. **Restart Cycle**: Enter the next iteration with new assumptions

### Case
Instagram's evolution path perfectly demonstrates the value of the feedback loop:
- **Build**: Initially was Burbn, a feature-rich location-based social app
- **Measure**: Discovered users only reacted strongly to the photo filter feature
- **Learn**: Location check-in and gaming features had extremely low usage
- **Pivot**: Abandoned other features, focused on photo sharing
- **Result**: Instagram was born, gaining 1 million users within 3 months

### Combination Tips
- **+ Lean Canvas**: Use the Lean Canvas to identify key assumptions that need verification, then test them one by one with the feedback loop
- **+ Affordable Loss Principle**: Set loss boundaries within each cycle to control experimental risks
- **+ Innovation Accounting**: Provide a quantitative indicator framework for the measurement phase to ensure objective evaluation

## Effectuation vs Causation

**Source**
Sarasvathy, S. D. (2001). "Causation and Effectuation: Toward a Theoretical Shift from Economic Inevitability to Entrepreneurial Contingency." *Academy of Management Review*, 26(2), 243-263.
Read, S., Dew, N., Sarasvathy, S. D., Song, M., & Wiltbank, R. (2016). *Effectuation: How Entrepreneurs Actually Make Decisions*. Edward Elgar Publishing.

### Definition
Effectuation and Causation represent two fundamentally different entrepreneurial decision-making logics: **Causal reasoning** starts from goals and works backwards to deduce the resources and steps needed to achieve the goals; **Effectual reasoning** starts from existing means and creates the future by controlling unexpected events and establishing strategic partnerships. Effectuation theory reveals how expert entrepreneurs make decisions in highly uncertain environments.

### Visual Structure
```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│          Causation vs Effectuation Decision Logics          │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                             │
│  Causation                  Effectuation                    │
│  ┌─────────────┐            ┌─────────────┐                │
│  │   Goal      │            │  Bird-in-   │                │
│  │   Setting   │            │   Hand      │                │
│  │      ↓      │            │      ↓      │                │
│  │  Resource   │            │  Affordable │                │
│  │  Matching   │            │    Loss     │                │
│  │      ↓      │            │      ↓      │                │
│  │   Plan      │            │  Lemonade   │                │
│  │  Execution  │            │  Principle  │                │
│  │      ↓      │            │      ↓      │                │
│  │  Achieve    │            │  Patchwork  │                │
│  │   Goal      │            │   Quilt     │                │
│  └─────────────┘            │      ↓      │                │
│                             │  Keep       │                │
│                             │  Options    │                │
│                             │  Open       │                │
│                             └─────────────┘                │
│                                                             │
│  Applicable:                 Applicable:                    │
│  ✓ Clear goals              ✓ Highly uncertain environments│
│  ✓ Resources available      ✓ Markets do not yet exist     │
│  ✓ Relatively stable        ✓ Resource constrained         │
│    environment                                               │
│                                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Effectuation's Five Principles

| Principle | Core Idea | Practical Implications |
|-----------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Bird-in-Hand** | Start from existing resources, capabilities, connections | First inventory existing assets, then find opportunities |
| **Lemonade Principle** | Turn surprises into opportunities | Treat surprises as learning opportunities when they occur |
| **Affordable Loss** | Take risks within controllable boundaries | Set maximum loss boundaries rather than pursuing maximum returns |
| **Patchwork Quilt Principle** | Expand capability boundaries through strategic partnerships | Cooperate rather than develop alone, reduce risks |
| **Keep Options Open** | Postpone major commitments, maintain flexibility | Do not bet on a single path before uncertainty is resolved |

### Application Steps
1. **Evaluate Context**: Judge the uncertainty level of the current environment and resource situation
2. **Inventory Means**: List owned resources, capabilities, connections, and knowledge
3. **Set Boundaries**: Determine the maximum affordable loss (including time, money, reputation)
4. **Build Partnerships**: Identify potential partners who can supplement capability gaps
5. **Seize Surprises**: When surprises occur, evaluate whether they are signals of new opportunities
6. **Delay Commitments**: Maintain multiple options when uncertainty is high, do not rush to bet

### Case
SpaceX's Effectuation decision-making path:
- **Bird-in-Hand**: Elon Musk started based on aerospace engineering knowledge and Silicon Valley resources
- **Affordable Loss**: Set a loss ceiling of hundreds of millions of dollars in the early days rather than at all costs
- **Lemonade Principle**: Rocket launch failures were transformed into valuable test data and PR opportunities
- **Patchwork Quilt**: Partnered with NASA to obtain technical support and contracts rather than bearing all R&D alone
- **Keep Options**: Simultaneously developed the Falcon series and Dragon spacecraft to diversify risks

### Combination Tips
- **+ Lean Canvas**: Design the Lean Canvas starting from Bird-in-Hand rather than from grand visions
- **+ Business Model Canvas**: Use the Patchwork Quilt Principle to design key partnerships
- **+ Crossing the Chasm**: Prioritize using Effectuation in highly uncertain early markets, can switch to Causation after entering the mainstream market

## Crossing the Chasm

**Source**
Moore, G. A. (1991). *Crossing the Chasm: Marketing and Selling High-Tech Products to Mainstream Customers*. HarperBusiness.

### Definition
The Crossing the Chasm model describes the unique challenges of marketing high-tech products. Based on Rogers' Technology Adoption Lifecycle theory, Moore discovered there is a "chasm" between early adopters and the early majority, which is the stage with the highest failure rate for high-tech products. The core strategy is to choose a niche market as a "beachhead," concentrate all resources to first establish dominance in this market, and then expand to adjacent markets.

### Visual Structure
```
Technology Adoption Lifecycle

Innovators   Early Adopters      ┌────── Chasm ──────┐   Early Majority   Late Majority   Laggards
    │           │                │                    │        │            │         │
    ▼           ▼                ▼                    ▼        ▼            ▼         ▼
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  2.5%        13.5%              │                    │   34%        34%          16%
                          Early Market                    Mainstream Market

                          ▼  Transition  ▼
    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  Beachhead Strategy (Bowling Alley Strategy)                      │
    │                                                                  │
    │   Phase 1: Capture a niche market (vertical market)              │
    │           ▼                                                      │
    │   Phase 2: Enter adjacent niche markets after dominating it      │
    │           (vertical expansion)                                   │
    │           ▼                                                      │
    │   Phase 3: Continue expanding until occupying the mainstream     │
    │           market                                                 │
    └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Application Steps
1. **Choose Beachhead**: Identify a sufficiently specific niche market with an urgent problem and a clear purchase decision-maker
2. **Build Whole Product**: Ensure the product can fully meet all needs of this niche market
3. **Position in Customer Language**: Shift from technical value to business value, explaining customer business outcomes
4. **Establish Dominance**: Spare no expense to gain absolute leading position (> 50% share) in the target market
5. **Vertical Expansion**: Replicate the successful model in adjacent niche markets
6. **Horizontal Expansion**: Enter the broader mainstream market

### Case
Salesforce's crossing path:
- **Beachhead**: Selected the SMB market, using the SaaS model to meet their CRM needs
- **Challenge**: Traditional software (such as Siebel) was expensive and complex to implement, unaffordable for SMBs
- **Strategy**: Attracted early adopters with the value proposition of "disrupting traditional software"
- **Expansion Path**: SMBs → Mid-market → Enterprise → Platform ecosystem
- **Success Factor**: Established dominance in each niche market before expanding

### Combination Tips
- **+ Lean Canvas**: Use the Lean Canvas to analyze the specific characteristics and pain points of the beachhead market
- **+ Disruptive Innovation**: Identify niche markets ignored by the mainstream market as entry points
- **+ Effectuation**: Maintain flexibility when entering the beachhead, adjusting strategies based on market feedback

## Model Combination Application Guide

### Entrepreneurial Stage × Mental Models

| Stage | Core Models | Auxiliary Models |
|-------|-------------|------------------|
| **Opportunity Identification** | Effectuation (Bird-in-Hand) | Disruptive Innovation Identification |
| **Business Model Design** | Lean Canvas | Business Model Canvas (Extended Version) |
| **Product Development** | Build-Measure-Learn Loop | MVP Principle |
| **Market Entry** | Crossing the Chasm | Lean Canvas (Channel Analysis) |
| **Large-Scale Growth** | Business Model Canvas | Rapid Decision-Making Mechanism |

### Recommended Model Combinations

**Combination 1: Lean Startup Package**
Lean Canvas + Build-Measure-Learn Loop + Affordable Loss Principle
Applicable to: Early-stage entrepreneurial teams with limited resources

**Combination 2: Market Breakthrough Package**
Crossing the Chasm + Disruptive Innovation + Effectuation
Applicable to: High-tech product commercialization

**Combination 3: Strategic Planning Package**
Business Model Canvas + Effectuation + Rapid Decision-Making Mechanism
Applicable to: Business model transformation and expansion decisions
