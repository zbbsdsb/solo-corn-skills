# Mental Model Library

> This document collects 20+ core mental models, divided into four categories: decision-making, strategic analysis, product & growth, and organizational management. Each model comes from 50 years of interdisciplinary research and can be directly applied to strategic decision-making, product development, market analysis, and team management for solo entrepreneurs.

---

## 1. Decision-Making Models (5)

### 1.1 First Principles Thinking

**Source**: Aristotle + Elon Musk's application

**Definition**: Start from the most basic, irreducible truths and rethink problems from scratch, rather than relying on analogies or conventional wisdom.

```
Traditional thinking: Others do it this way, so we do too
First principles: Start from physics laws, ask "Why must it be this way?"
```

**Application Steps**:
1. Identify current assumptions: What default premises does this problem have?
2. Break down to basic truths: What are provable facts?
3. Rebuild path: Construct new solutions from new facts
4. Verify feasibility: Is the new solution physically/logically feasible?

**Example**: Musk analyzed battery costs starting from raw material costs (lithium, cobalt, nickel) instead of referencing existing battery prices, redesigning Tesla's battery strategy.

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + Second-Order Thinking: Evaluate consequences of the rebuilt solution
- + Inversion: Assume success, reverse-engineer necessary conditions
- + Lean Canvas: Verify commercial feasibility of the rebuilt solution

---

### 1.2 Second-Order Thinking

**Source**: Howard Marks, "The Most Important Thing"

**Definition**: Consider second, third-order consequences of actions, not just immediate results.

```
First-order thinking: What's the benefit of doing this?
Second-order thinking: Then what? Then what? What unexpected consequences?
```

**Application Steps**:
1. Identify first-order effects: What are direct results of the action?
2. Identify second-order effects: What are long-term impacts of these results?
3. Identify third-order effects: What will second-order effects trigger?
4. Weigh decisions: Is it still worth doing after considering all consequences?

**Example**: Price promotion (1st: Sales ↑) → Brand positioning decline (2nd) → Future pricing difficulties (3rd)

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + First Principles: Deeply analyze root causes of consequences
- + Map is Not Territory: Mark uncertainty of second-order thinking
- + Minimize Surprise: Specifically analyze negative second-order effects

---

### 1.3 Inversion

**Source**: Mathematician Jacobi + Charlie Munger

**Definition**: Reverse-engineer necessary conditions from the goal, rather than forward-reasoning from the starting point.

```
Forward thinking: What do I have now → What can I do?
Inversion: What do I want → What conditions are needed?
```

**Application Steps**:
1. Clarify goal: What final state do we want to achieve?
2. Identify prerequisites: What necessary conditions are needed to achieve the goal?
3. Identify prerequisites of prerequisites: What do these conditions themselves require?
4. Act backwards: What can we do now to satisfy the nearest prerequisite?

**Example**: Amazon AWS - "If I want to build the best infrastructure, what do I need?" → Reversed to needing scale, cost structure, technical capability

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + First Principles: Ensure reverse derivation is based on correct first principles
- + Lean Canvas: Verify commercial closed-loop of reverse derivation
- + OKR: Set key results for the goal

---

### 1.4 The Map is Not the Territory

**Source**: Korzybski

**Definition**: Models, maps, theories are simplifications of reality, not reality itself. Be aware of model limitations.

```
Map ≠ Territory
Model ≠ Reality
Theory ≠ Truth
```

**Application Steps**:
1. Identify current model in use: What framework am I using to analyze?
2. Mark simplifications: What does the model ignore?
3. Look for counterexamples: What facts don't fit this model?
4. Look for alternative models: Are there other explanatory frameworks?
5. Stay humble: Accept that the model might be wrong

**Example**: Traditional MBA case studies assume "management determines success", but Dropbox, Instagram created huge value with small teams → Simplified timing, luck, technical moats

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + SWOT: Clarify limitations of each analysis dimension
- + Porter's Five Forces: Think if a sixth force is missing
- + AARRR: Think if a sixth stage is missing

---

### 1.5 Minimize Surprise

**Source**: Ray Dalio, "Principles"

**Definition**: When making decisions, consider possible worst outcomes and design mechanisms to prevent catastrophic consequences.

```
Standard question: Can this decision withstand the worst outcome?
Improved question: Can this decision prevent the worst outcome from happening?
```

**Application Steps**:
1. Brainstorm worst outcomes
2. Assess probability and impact
3. Design prevention mechanisms
4. Design emergency response
5. Set trigger conditions

**Example**: Startups shouldn't put all money into one channel, but should spread tests and learn quickly

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + Second-Order Thinking: Focus on analyzing negative second-order effects
- + Unit Economics: Ensure not losing more than can be afforded
- + Lean Canvas: Verify biggest risks with minimal cost

## 2. Strategic Analysis Models (5)

### 2.1 SWOT Analysis

**Source**: Albert Humphrey, Stanford Research Institute, 1960s-1970s

**Definition**: Systematically analyze internal and external environments, identify strategic opportunities and threats.

```
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
│   Strengths     │   Weaknesses    │
│   Internal      │   Internal      │
├─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
│   Opportunities │   Threats       │
│   External      │   External      │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

**Application Steps**:
1. List Strengths: What do customers value? What unique capabilities?
2. List Weaknesses: Resource gaps? Capability shortcomings?
3. List Opportunities: Market trends? Policy changes? Technological progress?
4. List Threats: Competitors? New entrants? Substitutes?
5. Combination analysis: SO, WO, ST, WT strategies

**Example**: Apple entering iPhone era
- S: Brand, design, ecosystem
- W: Carrier relationships, closed system
- O: Mobile internet explosion
- T: Google Android open alliance

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + Porter's Five Forces: Deeply analyze external environment
- + Map is Not Territory: Limitations of SWOT
- + Ansoff Matrix: Choose growth strategy based on SWOT

---

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Source**: Michael Porter, Harvard Business Review, 1979

**Definition**: Analyze five forces of industry competitive structure, determining industry profitability.

```
                    Threat of New Entrants
                        ↓
                ┌───────────────┐
Supplier Power → │   Industry    │ ← Buyer Power
                │  Competition  │
                └───────────────┘
                        ↑
                Threat of Substitutes
```

**Five Forces**:
1. **Threat of New Entrants**: Barrier height? Economies of scale? Brand loyalty?
2. **Supplier Power**: Concentration? Alternative suppliers? Switching costs?
3. **Buyer Power**: Concentration? Information transparency? Product differentiation?
4. **Threat of Substitutes**: Substitute value-for-money? User switching costs?
5. **Industry Competition**: Number of competitors? Industry concentration? Differentiation level?

**Application Steps**:
1. Evaluate strength of each force
2. Identify most critical forces
3. Analyze force change trends
4. Design response strategies: Enhance defense or exploit changes

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + SWOT: External force analysis is basis for SWOT-O/T
- + PEST: Macro environment shapes five forces
- + Value Chain Analysis: Understand source of competitive advantage

### 2.3 Ansoff Matrix

**Source**: Igor Ansoff, 1957

**Definition**: Define four growth strategies based on product/market dimensions.

```
                    Product
              New       Existing
            ┌────────┬────────┐
        New │ Diversification │ Market  │
    Market  │                │ Penetration
            ├────────┼────────┤
            │ Product │ Market  │
   Existing │ Development│ Focus
            └────────┴────────┘
```

**Four Strategies**:
1. **Market Penetration**: Existing product → Existing market → Increase share
2. **Market Development**: Existing product → New market → Geographic/demographic expansion
3. **Product Development**: New product → Existing market → Product line expansion
4. **Diversification**: New product → New market → New track

**Risk Level**: Market Penetration < Market Development < Product Development < Diversification

**Application Steps**:
1. Assess current product-market position
2. Identify growth goals
3. Evaluate risk of each quadrant
4. Choose strategic path
5. Develop implementation plan

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + Lean Canvas: Verify assumptions of new strategy
- + Unit Economics: Ensure financial feasibility of strategy
- + Porter's Five Forces: Evaluate attractiveness of entering new market

---

### 2.4 PEST Analysis

**Source**: Francis J. Aguilar, 1967

**Definition**: Analyze macro environment impact on business, identify opportunities and threats.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│         Political                   │
│  Policy, regulation, government stability, trade policy  │
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│         Economic                    │
│  Growth, interest rates, inflation, exchange rates, consumer power  │
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│         Social                      │
│  Demographics, culture, values, lifestyle, education  │
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│      Technological                  │
│  Innovation, R&D, tech obsolescence, automation, digitalization  │
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Extended Version**: PESTEL (adds Legal and Environmental)

**Application Steps**:
1. Collect key information for each dimension
2. Identify trends and changes
3. Assess potential impact on business
4. Distinguish opportunities and threats
5. Develop response strategies

**Example**: Pre-pandemic PEST analysis predicted remote work trend

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + Porter's Five Forces: PEST shapes five forces
- + SWOT: PEST is external basis for S/T
- + User Journey Map: Identify macro changes' impact on users

### 2.5 Value Chain Analysis

**Source**: Michael Porter, 1985

**Definition**: Decompose enterprise activities into support activities and primary activities, identify source of competitive advantage.

```
                    Firm Infrastructure
                        ↓
                ┌───────────────┐
                │   Human       │
                │  Resources    │
                └───────────────┘
                        ↓
                ┌───────────────┐
                │   Technology  │
                │  Development  │
                └───────────────┘
                        ↓
                ┌───────────────┐
                │   Procurement │
                └───────────────┘
                        ↓
┌──────────────────────────────────────┐
│           Primary Activities          │
│  Inbound → Operations → Outbound → Marketing → Service  │
└──────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Application Steps**:
1. Decompose enterprise activities into support and primary activities
2. Identify cost and value of each activity
3. Compare differences with competitors
4. Identify links that can create differentiation advantage
5. Decide insourcing, outsourcing, or partnership

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + Porter's Five Forces: Understand how value chain affects five forces
- + Unit Economics: Evaluate economics of each activity
- + SWOT: Identify strengths and weaknesses of value chain

## 3. Product & Growth Models (5)

### 3.1 Lean Canvas

**Source**: Ash Maurya, 2010, based on Lean Startup

**Definition**: Simplified business canvas for early-stage startups, focused on risk validation.

```
┌─────────────┬─────────────┬─────────────┐
│   Problem   │  Solution   │ Unique Value │
│ 1-3 items   │ 1-3 items   │  Proposition │
├─────────────┼─────────────┼─────────────┤
│   Key Metrics│ Unfair Advantage│ Channels   │
│             │             │             │
├─────────────┴─────────────┼─────────────┤
│         Cost Structure     │  Revenue   │
│  Customer Acquisition + Delivery│ Model    │
└───────────────────────────┴─────────────┘
```

**Four Quadrants**:
- **Problem**: Top 3 problems
- **Solution**: Top 3 features
- **Unfair Advantage**: Can't be easily copied or bought
- **Key Metrics**: Activities you measure

**Application Steps**:
1. List Top 3 problems
2. Propose Top 3 solutions
3. Define unique value proposition
4. Determine key metrics
5. Verify unfair advantage
6. Design channels
7. Verify cost structure and revenue model

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + First Principles: Dig to root cause of problems
- + Unit Economics: Verify LTV > CAC
- + AARRR: Design acquisition and monetization path

### 3.2 Business Model Canvas

**Source**: Alexander Osterwalder & Yves Pigneur, 2010

**Definition**: Nine building blocks describing how a business creates, delivers, and captures value.

```
┌────────────┬────────────┬────────────┬────────────┬────────────┐
│  Key Partners │ Key Activities │ Value Proposition │ Customer Relationships │ Customer Segments │
├────────────┴────────────┴────────────┴────────────┴────────────┤
│                    Cost Structure              │      Revenue Streams    │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┴───────────────────┘
```

**Nine Building Blocks**:
1. Customer Segments
2. Value Proposition
3. Channels
4. Customer Relationships
5. Revenue Streams
6. Key Resources
7. Key Activities
8. Key Partnerships
9. Cost Structure

**Application Steps**:
1. Define customer segments
2. Design value proposition
3. Plan channels
4. Build customer relationships
5. Design revenue streams
6. Identify key resources
7. Determine key activities
8. Evaluate partnerships
9. Calculate cost structure

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + Lean Canvas: Lean Canvas is early version, Business Model Canvas is mature version
- + Unit Economics: Verify economic logic of each block
- + Porter's Five Forces: Understand external environment of business model

### 3.3 AARRR Funnel

**Source**: Dave McClure, 2007, Startup Metrics for Pirates

**Definition**: Five key metrics of user lifecycle.

```
Acquisition
      ↓
Activation
      ↓
Retention
      ↓
Revenue
      ↓
Referral
```

**Key Questions for Each Stage**:
1. **Acquisition**: Where do users come from?
2. **Activation**: Is user's first experience good?
3. **Retention**: Will users come back?
4. **Revenue**: Will users pay?
5. **Referral**: Will users recommend others?

**Application Steps**:
1. Define key metrics for each stage
2. Plot current funnel data
3. Identify biggest drop-off point
4. Prioritize testing improvements to biggest drop-off
5. Repeat test-measure-learn cycle

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + North Star Metric: Focus on core metric
- + Lean Canvas: Verify assumptions of each stage
- + User Journey Map: Understand user experience at each stage

---

### 3.4 North Star Metric

**Source**: Sean Ellis, GrowthHackers

**Definition**: Single most important metric reflecting core value created for users.

**Characteristics of a Good North Star Metric**:
- ✅ Reflects user value
- ✅ Measurable
- ✅ Growth-driving
- ✅ Leading indicator, not lagging
- ✅ Understandable across company

**Common North Star Metrics**:
- Facebook: Daily Active Users
- Airbnb: Nights Booked
- Spotify: Monthly Active Users + Listening Time
- Slack: Daily Active Users + Messages Sent

**Application Steps**:
1. List core value users get from product
2. Find metric that measures this value
3. Ensure it's a leading indicator
4. Test and iterate
5. Align entire company around it

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + AARRR: North Star Metric drives goals for each stage
- + OKR: North Star Metric is company-level Objective
- + Unit Economics: Ensure metric correlates with revenue

### 3.5 User Journey Map

**Source**: Adaptive Path + service design

**Definition**: Visualize the entire process a user goes through to achieve a goal.

```
Stage 1          Stage 2          Stage 3          Stage 4
[Awareness]  →    [Consideration] →  [Conversion]  →    [Retention]

Goal      Touchpoints      Behavior      Emotion      Pain Points      Opportunities
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
User      Sees      Browses      Hesitates      Price      Free
Wants     Ad        Features    Worries      Too High   Trial
XX        Catches   Compares    After-Sales  Can Save   Better
          Attention Several               Money      Understand
```

**Application Steps**:
1. Define user persona and goal
2. Divide into stages
3. Identify touchpoints for each stage
4. Record behavior, emotion, pain points
5. Discover opportunities
6. Improve experience

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + AARRR: User journey is refinement of AARRR
- + Lean Canvas: User pain points drive problem definition
- + Lean Canvas problem validation

## 4. Organizational Management Models (5)

### 4.1 OKR Framework

**Source**: Andy Grove (Intel) + John Doerr

**Definition**: Objectives and Key Results system to align team goals and track progress.

```
Objective: Where are we going?
    ↓
Key Results: How do we know we got there?
    ↓
Initiatives: What are we going to do?
```

**OKR Rules**:
- O is qualitative, inspiring
- KR is quantitative, measurable
- Typically 1 O + 3-5 KRs
- Score at end of cycle (0-1.0)
- 0.6-0.7 is good, don't aim for 1.0

**Application Steps**:
1. Set company-level O
2. Define company-level KRs
3. Team aligns to O and KRs
4. Individual aligns
5. Weekly progress check
6. End-of-cycle scoring and retrospective

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + North Star Metric: OKRs support North Star Metric
- + Lean Canvas: OKRs validate canvas assumptions
- + Unit Economics: KRs include economic metrics

---

### 4.2 Team Topologies

**Source**: Matthew Skelton & Manuel Pais, 2019

**Definition**: Design inter-team interaction patterns to maximize flow, efficiency, and trust.

**Four Fundamental Team Types**:
```
1. Stream-Aligned
   └── Main team delivering user value

2. Enabling
   └── Supports stream teams with specialized capabilities

3. Platform
   └── Provides self-service capabilities

4. Complicated Subsystem
   └── Handles complex technical decisions
```

**Four Interaction Modes**:
1. **Collaboration**: Need tight coordination
2. **X-as-a-Service**: Call a service
3. **Facilitation**: Help other teams
4. **Incubation**: Independent exploration

**Application Steps**:
1. Map current teams
2. Identify bottlenecks and dependencies
3. Choose interaction modes
4. Evolve gradually
5. Continuously optimize

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + OKR: Align team goals
- + Lead Time: Measure team effectiveness
- + Cognitive Load Management: Avoid team overload

### 4.3 MTF Analysis

**Source**: Starcraft strategic concept applied to business

**Definition**: Market-Technology-Team Fit analysis.

```
        ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
        │             Success Zone             │
        │  (Market Opportunity + Tech Feasible)│
        └─────────────────────────────────────┘
                    ↗           ↖
                    │  Big Market│
        Strong Tech │ Weak Tech  │ Strong Tech
        ────────────┼────────────┼─────────────
        Strong Tech │  Failure   │  Failure
                    │ (Weak Market)│ (Market+Team Weak)
                    │            │
                    └────────────┘

Team
```

**Three Dimensions**:
1. **Market Fit**: Is there real market demand?
2. **Technology Fit**: Is technology mature and feasible?
3. **Team Fit**: Does the team have capability to execute?

**Application Steps**:
1. Assess market opportunity size
2. Assess technical feasibility
3. Assess team capability
4. Identify mismatched dimensions
5. Develop improvement plan or pivot

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + Lean Canvas: Verify market-product fit
- + First Principles: Evaluate technical first principles
- + Porter's Five Forces: Assess market attractiveness

### 4.4 Unit Economics

**Source**: Einstein Ventures + SaaS industry best practices

**Definition**: Analyze economics of a single business unit to ensure business model sustainability.

**Core Metrics**:
```
LTV (Lifetime Value): Lifetime value of a single user
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost): Cost to acquire a single customer
LTV > 3 × CAC: Healthy standard
```

**Calculation Formulas**:
```
ARPU = Monthly Revenue / Number of Users
LTV = ARPU × Gross Margin × Average Lifespan
CAC = Total Marketing Cost / New Users
Payback Period = CAC / (ARPU × Gross Margin)
```

**Application Steps**:
1. Calculate ARPU
2. Calculate gross margin
3. Estimate average lifespan
4. Calculate LTV
5. Calculate CAC
6. Calculate LTV:CAC
7. Optimize unit economics

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + Lean Canvas: Verify economic model
- + AARRR: Optimize full link from acquisition to monetization
- + North Star Metric: Choose metric that drives unit economics

### 4.5 Impact Mapping

**Source**: Gojko Adzic

**Definition**: Visual mapping from goal to action to ensure doing the right things.

```
Why (Goal)      Who (Actor)      How (Impact)      What (Deliverable)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                        Impact            Deliverable
                        ────            ─────
Business Goal ←─── Actor 1 ←─── Behavior Change ←─── Feature/Activity
                │
                └─── Actor 2 ←─── Behavior Change ←─── Feature/Activity
```

**Four Layers**:
1. **Why**: What business goal do we want to achieve?
2. **Who**: Who needs to change behavior?
3. **How**: What behavior change is needed?
4. **What**: What do we need to deliver?

**Application Steps**:
1. Define business goal (SMART)
2. Identify actors needing behavior change
3. List needed behavior changes
4. Define needed deliverables
5. Verify alignment
6. Prioritize

**Combination Suggestions**:
- + OKR: Impact Mapping is tactical tool for OKRs
- + User Journey Map: Think about impact from user perspective
- + Lean Canvas: Verify goal and deliverables
